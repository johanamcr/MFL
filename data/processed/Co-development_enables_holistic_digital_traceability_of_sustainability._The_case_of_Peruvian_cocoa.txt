Agroecological TRANSITIONS Program Policy Brief February 2025 Co-development enables holistic digital traceability of sustainability The case of Peruvian cocoa Francisco Hidalgo, Sarah K. Jones, Andrea Sánchez, Yovita Ivanova, José Sánchez-Choy, Jonathan Mockshell, and Jonathan Steinke Key messages Policy shifts toward agroecology and growing consumer awareness have made holistic sustainability monitoring increasingly relevant. Advancements in digital technologies create favorable conditions for developing digital traceability tools to respond to this need. Digital traceability can facilitate data collection on agroecological metrics and end-to-end traceability. Technologies such as blockchain can make information management more transparent, reliable, and safe. End-to-end traceability requires the cooperation of different stakeholders and a system that integrates their information needs and capabilities while providing indicators that can be used to assess agrifood value chain sustainability. Here, we show a co-development approach proved to be an effective way to meet these requirements. Co-development involved addressing participants’ diversity of perspectives, interests, and capabilities. Several challenges arose, which required adaptive planning. Translation and mediation strategies were effective in navigating this environment. The use of a compact, yet holistic, set of agroecological metrics, and implementation of iterative programming and testing cycles with end-users, were key attributes to achieving systems’ contextualization and applicability. Identifying effective incentives, and removing barriers, is key to ensuring participation in co-development processes. Enabling holistic digital traceability calls for financial sustainability schemes that do not burden producers, and practices for managing data responsibly. PSii Project 1. Enhancing value chain sustainability through end-to-end traceability Pressure on natural ecosystems keeps growing worldwide, in part due to agricultural expansion and intensification (Laurance et al., 2014). At the same time, contemporary agriculture is associated with grave social and economic challenges, including the precarious livelihoods of many smallholder farmers in the Global South (Wezel et al., 2020; Lähde et al., 2023). Against this background, agroecology has emerged as a holistic agrifood system paradigm that aims at realigning agricultural production with concerns for social, economic, and environmental sustainability (Bezner Kerr et al., 2021). The practice of agroecology is grounded in 13 principles (HLPE, 2019; Wezel et al., 2020) (Figure 1), that are focused on integrating ecological dynamics to improve the synergies between plants, animals, humans, and the environment, while also considering the social conditions necessary to build sustainable and fair food systems (Jones et al., 2022). Responding to the growing consumer demand for sustainably produced agri-food commodities, sustainability certification schemes, such as Organic, Fairtrade, UTZ, and Rainforest Alliance, have been established to verify the application of socially and environmentally responsible practices in agri-food value chains. The cocoa sector, which predominantly sources from smallholder farmers in tropical countries, largely relies on these certification schemes to monitor sustainability. This generally involves verification through independent audits of farmers’ and producers’ organizations. However, despite its relevant contributions, this system presents some limitations. Certification schemes typically prioritize a few aspects of sustainability, rather than recognizing sustainability as a multidimensional phenomenon. Additionally,

Ongoing changes in policy and consumer preferences appear to incentivize more holistic sustainability monitoring. Agroecology represents this holistic perspective well, encompassing multiple dimensions of sustainability at different scales, and following clearly defined principles. Hence, measuring agroecological metrics around cocoa value chains can be used to assess gradual progress towards sustainability. Additionally, it can meet consumer and regulatory monitoring requirements. Simultaneously, the growing availability of low-cost digital technologies has also made holistic monitoring more feasible for farmer organizations and other interested parties. Additionally, recent regulations such as the EU regulation on deforestation are further increasing interest in digital traceability among cocoa suppliers (Steinke et al., 2023). Considering the mentioned shortcomings and opportunities, we explored the “how to?” of establishing a digital traceability system for agroecology in the cocoa sector. Through a case study we explored how such a system can support tracing agroecological metrics from the farms of origin to the final chocolate bar. From the process we draw conclusions on how to holistically assess the sustainability in agri-food value chains.

3 Agroecological TRANSITIONS Policy Brief 2. Digital technologies for traceability To reduce the ‘entry costs’ for accessing premium sustainability markets, new digital technologies for tracing agricultural commodities from farm to fork have emerged. These systems allow eliciting data from farmers – for example, diverse agroecological metrics – using handheld devices, and enable subsequent tracking of batches of cocoa along the value chain. Digitalized systems avoid the need for cumbersome manual data collection and digitization. Once data is entered by farmers or their organizations, downstream stakeholders, including manufacturers, retailers, or consumers, can then verify the sustainability metrics associated with the product they buy. These systems reduce the need for costly on-site audits while offering consumers reliable and nuanced insights into product sustainability. Digital traceability systems often rely on Blockchain technology, where enumerated data – for example, on the socio-environmental conditions of farm production – is stored transparently online and cannot be modified retroactively by any stakeholder involved. With blockchain, transactions are not stored centrally, but rather distributed over a peer-to-peer network. To record, change, or remove data, the approval of all network participants is required. Digital traceability schemes allow secure storage of data while enabling decentralized verification, thereby enhancing transparency and trust between value chain stakeholders. Moreover, through digital traceability systems, it is possible to assess agroecology metrics at the landscape and food system scale by linking information from different farms and tracking it along the journey of cocoa from the farm to the consumer. 3. Co-development of digital traceability in context. The case of Peruvian cocoa In this case study, we explored how the differing needs, priorities, and resources of upstream and downstream stakeholders can be addressed by a single traceability system. To this end, we engaged a cocoa cooperative in Peru, a cocoa buyer and chocolate manufacturer in France, and a technological start-up in Italy, to co-design and experimentally set up a digital traceability system for a cocoa value chain. 3.1. The partnership The partnership in this study included the cocoa cooperative Colpa de Loros, the manufacturer Kaoka, the IT company Trusty, and the research organization Alliance of Bioversity International and CIAT. Colpa de Loros is a cooperative of cocoa farmers in the Ucayali region of Peru (Figure 2). This cooperative is the main cocoa exporting company in the region. It includes 451 smallholder farmers, who – assisted by the cooperative management and technical staff – comply with the certification requirements for EU organic, Fairtrade, and currently in the process of certification with Rainforest Alliance and compliance with the EU regulation Data collection in a cocoa farm in Ucayali - Peru CIAT/Francisco Hidalgo on deforestation-free supply chains. Cooperative management had expressed their expectation for using a digital solution to streamline and facilitate the cumbersome processes of data collection for informing their operations, socio-environmental projects on terrain, and reporting for the different sustainability certifications. Kaoka is a manufacturer of fine organic chocolate primarily targeting the French market. Due to the EU regulation on deforestation-free supply chains, the company is required to implement a digital traceability system. In addition to tracing cocoa beans to the origin – to verify absence of deforestation –, Kaoka management has expressed seeing added value in tracing agroecological metrics as part of transparent corporate social responsibility efforts. Trusty is an IT service provider offering a ready-made digital system for traceability, including: offline data collection on farm and along the supply chain, centralized data overview for tracing cocoa batches along the supply chain (data secured through Blockchain technology), and QR code functionality to make tracing data about cocoa available to consumers. Alliance of Bioversity International and CIAT is an international research for development institute. Researchers worked to facilitate the analysis of requirements and moderated the co- design process involving the other three stakeholders. This included providing initial training on digital tool use to Colpa de Loros technicians, communicating required adaptations to Trusty, and evaluating the pilot implementation. This partnership aimed to build a digital traceability system that incorporated agroecology-sensitive performance metrics, adapted to cocoa farming in Peru’s Ucayali region.

4 Agroecological TRANSITIONS Policy Brief 3.2. Co-designing the traceability system Through bilateral conversations, group conversations, and participation in a one-day workshop in Peru in April 2023 with leadership and field technicians of Colpa de Loros, the partnership worked to understand the needs, resources, and capacities of key stakeholders (farmers and technicians in the Colpa de Loros cooperative, and Kaoka) related to establishing a holistic digital traceability system. These deliberations led to stakeholders agreeing that the system had to provide added value to both Colpa de Loros and Kaoka, as buy-in and active participation were needed from both upstream and downstream supply chain stakeholders to ensure smooth and sustained implementation. Figure 2. Area of study. Stakeholders agreed that the monitoring system should ideally address the following needs: 1) the possibility to include forest cover monitoring, responding to Kaoka’s need to comply with the EU regulation, and 2) highly customizable offline data collection, responding to Colpa de Loros’ need to make data collection on socio-environmental variables more efficient and reliable. Given its possibilities of building easily customizable digital questionnaires linked to Blockchain technology, Trusty was identified as one digital platform where such tool could be designed. Address the cooperatives’ information needs Colpa de Loros’ information needs focused on tracking multidimensional socio-environmental factors of production, farms, and households. In response, the Trusty digital survey was developed to collect information on details about cocoa plots (e.g. varieties grown, ages of plants, number of plants and areas), status of other crops and livestock grown on-farm, socio-economic characteristics of the farmers’ family, conservation practices, land tenure, and agricultural practices (soil protection, fertilization, plagues and diseases control, etc.). 2. Single data collection interface To avoid duplication of survey efforts, all data collection paper-based forms of the cooperative were integrated into one single digital interface, generating a single digital survey. Digitizing the data collection by cooperative technicians was expected to minimize drudgery and increase data quality. 3. Comprehensive data on agroecological metrics To provide information for multiple dimensions of agroecology, a holistic, yet simple, set of metrics was agreed between partners (Figure 3). The iterative and collaborative selection process followed four key considerations: a) Comprehensiveness: a broad diversity of agroecology dimensions is covered, b) Reliability: the metrics should not require in-depth training or advanced skills for accurate assessment, c) Time efficiency: the data collection should require minimal time-effort for enumeration by cooperative technicians, d) Clarity: the metrics should be easily communicable and understandable by end consumers, as part of Kaoka’s communication on CSR efforts. See the Annex for further details on the agroecology-sensitive metrics included in the traceability system. 4. Compliance with EU regulation on deforestation The system should allow the collection of field data and a traceability system to respond to information requirements to comply with the recent EU regulation on deforestation. This includes reliable information of origin and tracking the movement of cocoa along the value chain. CIAT/Elizabeth Ramírez Pérez

Field testing the system A testing phase was conducted in the field once an initial prototype of the digital system for data collection of agroecology metrics was set. An iterative, participatory testing approach was used over the course of 20 days, consisting of co-design sessions, on- farm testing, and training sessions. This phase aimed to adapt the system to the local context and the cooperative’s requirements and aspirations, assess its performance under real conditions, and familiarize the cooperative’s staff with the technology. 3.3.1. Co-design sessions Through iterative co-design sessions with cooperative technical staff, we gained a better understanding of the current socio- environmental information system - heavily reliant on paperwork –, obtained feedback on prototypes of the digital system, integrated the cooperative’s additional information requirements, and oriented the phrasing of questions for easy and unambiguous enumeration. 3.3.2. On-farm testing Prototypes of the system were tested on farms. The tests resulted in improvements to the system in terms of contextualization and usability. The activity provided an opportunity for the cooperative’s technical staff to gain familiarity with the technology. On-farm testing, co-design sessions, and programming phases were combined through agile iterative cycles. 3.3.3. Training sessions Training sessions were conducted with the cooperative’s technical staff on the use of the technology. These sessions were assisted by a step-by-step user guide. This resource can also be used to train additional staff in the future and for consultation. Figure 3. Selected metrics and correspondence with agroecology principles. 7 Agroecological TRANSITIONS Policy Brief 4. Implementing translation and mediation for collaboration To enable collaborative work, co-development involved processes of translation and mediation. We describe these social dynamics and the implementation of the two processes in the Peruvian cocoa case below. 4.1. Translation Translation involves rendering codes of communication from one party understandable to the other. It can be literal, as when different languages are spoken, but also metaphorical, as when parties hold different worldviews and use different jargons. Translation was implemented in multiple instances during the study given divergences in these elements. For example, in the case of Kaoka, the translation process mainly involved getting this company familiar with agroecology and monitoring systems. The translation process facilitated contextualization and the interpretation of the digital system by the cooperative. 4.2. Mediation This step involves allowing representation of multiple interests and perspectives held by the different parties. It also entails addressing differences and mechanisms of conflict resolution. The mediation process in this study consisted of understanding the diverging expectations of the participants and incorporating them into the system. This was done through one-to-one and multilateral meetings. The working conditions in the field created challenges for the cooperative and produced friction and discontinuity in the process. For example, cacao farms are widely dispersed, the cooperative is understaffed, and road infrastructure is poor. Additionally, the cooperative has prioritized other activities and had to deal with a drought that severely affected production. We used mediation to understand and work around these challenges. This was done by adapting the process to the cooperative’s capabilities and ensuring the main concerns for each stakeholder group were well documented and shared with all participants. Based on the experience in the cocoa sector in Peru, we propose the following seven steps are followed to replicate the process of co-developing a digital system in the Vietnamese rice sector. Alternatively, these steps can serve as guidelines for replicating the process elsewhere. 1. Identify potential partners Potential partners can be identified through a quick exploration of the social network associated with agroecology. These partners can come from the production sector (e.g. farmers and cooperatives), industry (agricultural and technological), research, government, and the third sector. The International Rice Research Institute (IRRI) stands as one of the most relevant actors in the development of the rice value chain in Vietnam. Additionally, Association of Food Transparency (AFT), Vietnam Digital Agriculture Association (VIDA), and Vietnam Organic Agriculture Association (VOAA), have been identified as relevant platforms for improving traceability and digital agricultural transformation (Mihn et al., 2024). 2. Identify sustainability indicators currently monitored This activity is relevant to identifying what metrics can be assessed with the existing information and the remaining gaps. Also, this activity assists in understanding the information needs of the partners that should be included in the system. Sustainability standards are less prominent in rice than in crops like coffee or cocoa. Therefore, it can be expected to have a more limited availability of data for monitoring sustainability indicators in this value chain. 3. Characterize the current systems for data collection and actors involved This activity aims to make an inventory of the systems currently used for collecting and analyzing data on agroecological metrics. Furthermore, to identify and describe the actors involved in data collection and analysis. The result is an understanding of the systems that can be adapted for the new traceability system. In addition, it identifies the primary and secondary users. This information is helpful to understand contextualization and capacity building requirements. 8 Agroecological TRANSITIONS Policy Brief 5. Looking forward: Building holistic digital traceability in new contexts Our co-design approach contributed to the contextualization and applicability of the digital system. However, the process was not without challenges. To enable collaborative work, translation and mediation strategies were needed to address the diversity of perspectives and interests among participants. Using a participatory approach enabled the cocoa cooperative to become familiar with the technology while bringing the technology closer to its capabilities, yet focused training activities were essential for building the local skills and knowledge needed to effectively use these tools. We identified three attributes of success in the co-development of an effective traceability tool: consideration of the needs and motivations of all potential users, the use of a compact, yet holistic, set of agroecological metrics, and iteratively testing the tool with end-users. Iterative agile programming and testing cycles proved essential for contextualization and applicability. 4. Identify skills and infrastructure requirements for strengthening capacities for using digital technologies This activity sought to identify digital literacy and technological infrastructure gaps in the context of Vietnam rice production in order to contextualize the system to current conditions and orient activities that contribute to closing the gap (e.g. providing smartphones or training to technical staff). 5. Setting financial and incentives schemes Investments may come from different sources (e.g. increased sales or bonus prices for agroecological products). It is important not to place the burden on farmers and cooperatives. Compliance with the EU regulation on deforestation-free value chains proved to be an influential investment incentive in the present case, as it is about to become a condition for accessing the lucrative EU market. Increasing the efficiency and reliability of the cooperative’s processes was key to building their interest in the system. It would be crucial to identify equally relevant incentives in the case of Vietnam. 6. Co-development and testing Holistic metrics should be set jointly after considering key factors (completeness, reliability, efficiency, and clarity). Metrics developed for the Peruvian cocoa case can be adapted. However, it may be necessary to set additional contextualized metrics. A co-development approach and piloting the system under real conditions will potentially increase its acceptability and effectiveness. 7. Make effective and responsible use of data Integrating the requirements and perspectives of the different parties into the system increases the likelihood that the data collected will be used effectively. Implementing mechanisms for responsible information management is crucial. This includes the protection of personal data, informed consent, and disclosing the use of data. We identified seven steps to follow to replicate this case study successfully in other contexts (see inset box). Important considerations within these steps include setting financial sustainability schemes that do not burden farmers and cooperatives, identify influential incentives for the participation of the different partners (these can vary from one group to another), and implement practices of responsible data management. Monitoring, adaptive learning, and sharing both successes and failures, can help to accelerate the global transition to digital supply chains. Adoption rates are commonly used to assess impact in the process of innovation and developmental interventions. This indicator alone, however, cannot capture the multidimensional outcomes of co-development. An outcome of the present case study was that end-users were actively driving the process, making sure their own needs and interests were represented. Monitoring end-user engagement and agency over each step of digital tool development could be added to the list of indicators future teams use to assess impact, to encourage true co-development and empower food system actors to use and improve the tools long after projects end.
