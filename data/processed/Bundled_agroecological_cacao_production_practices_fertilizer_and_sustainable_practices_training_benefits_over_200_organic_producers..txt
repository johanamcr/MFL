REPORTING 2024 EVIDENCE OUTCOME STORY/IMPACT STATEMENT Organic fertilizers boost yields, yet according to our research, most cacao producers in Ucayali region do not use fertilizers year-round, due to limited finances and knowledge. We trained 69 organic cacao producers, who are now adopting innovative bundled agroecological practices to significantly enhance their productivity. Bundles of practices include preparing and applying organic fertilizers to improve managing solid organic waste generated from cacao production. Expected scalable outcomes include enhanced soil health, recycling, cacao production, and incomes. The ingredients are selected for their high carbon-to- nitrogen ratio – a crucial factor in enhancing soil fertility [3,4, 5] – and are both affordable and readily available to producers. The proposed organic fertilizer or soil amendment is innovative, utilizing cacao by-products such as husks and mucilage to add value while improving soil fertility. This approach is particularly relevant given that the cacao industry generates significant by-products and waste [5]. Contributing external partners: Ucayali region, Peru. Sixty-nine trained organic cacao producers are adopting innovative practices to boost productivity. Traditional agricultural management practices do not prioritize fertilizer use and farmers lack funds to purchase fertilizer, which are spent on more pressing needs [4]. Incentives can support adopting agroecological practices, in this case: 1.Training on organic fertilizer (non-market incentive) 2.Potential cacao price increases from the private sector (market incentive) and ; 3.Key inputs to producers (treatment group) randomly selected for fertilizer preparation. Partnerships between the Alliance and Instituto Nacional de Innovación Agricola (INIA), producer organizations, and the private sector have supported adopting innovative bundled agroecological practices. Home-made organic fertilizer presents a viable, cost- effective solution for producers to enhance soil fertility. Bokashi is an organic, nutrient-rich fertilizer obtained from fermenting readily available dry materials [3,4,5,6]. Bokashi enriches the soil with essential nutrients, increasing microbial activity, and promoting growth of beneficial bacteria and fungi that help decompose organic matter [1,3,4]. Training on how to make and apply Bokashi was conducted by INIA experts and coordinated by Private sector and Incentives and Investments [PSii] research staff. Three training sessions were conducted for 69 cacao producers ‒ all members of the Banaqui, Colpa de Loros and Cocepasa cooperatives ‒ and for two extension agents. The training was divided into theoretical and practical sessions where producers “learned by doing” by making a batch of Bokashi. Scaling this innovation involves two main strategies: 1) providing manuals with detailed instructions on ingredient dosages and application methods, and 2) supplying randomly selected producers with key inputs such as organic fertilizers (minimum dose), sugar, yeast, and tools. INIA also shared a validated formula in the “learning-by-doing'' part. Producers implementing these practices could further disseminate knowledge, while soil and substrate samples were collected before and after applying Bokashi. This provides evidence to demonstrate its benefits and encourage broader regional adoption. ELABORATION OF OUTCOME/IMPACT STATEMENT Gender relevance: 1 - Significant. At least 25% of the participants in each training were women. The project promotes gender inclusion by designing family-friendly training environments and addressing barriers to participation, such as providing meals and creating a supportive atmosphere. This approach empowers women with knowledge and skills to improve agricultural productivity and household incomes, advancing their role in the cacao value chain [7]. Youth relevance: 1 - Significant. The project actively engages youth in training sessions, emphasizing practical, hands-on activities such as the production of Bokashi organic fertilizer. In each training session, young participants were the most interested in taking the reins for the practical part of the exercise [8,9]. Youth (aged 19-39) accounted for 28% of the participants. Capacity Development relevance: 2 - Principal. The theoretical part of the workshop was given by people who are well known in the community and who usually participate in the events organized by the cooperative with the intention of generating confidence among the other participants. By training farmers, extension agents, and cooperatives, the project builds individual and institutional capacity. It also strengthens local agricultural institutions, which can spread knowledge more widely [3,4,10]. Climate Change relevance: 2 - Principal. The project directly addresses climate resilience through its focus on soil health, recycling, and organic waste management. By training farmers to produce and use Bokashi fertilizer, the initiative promotes low-carbon farming practices that enhance soil fertility, reduce environmental degradation, and mitigate the effects of poor waste management on the environment [3,5,6]. Other crosscutting dimensions: The dimensions of environment, equity and the strengthening of soft skills such as leadership were reinforced. Special emphasis was placed on soil health and micro-nutrients that can contribute to strengthening the soil and which are easily accessible to farmers [3,5,6]. Similarly, the practical part was strengthened in terms of equity since the activities were divided in such a way that both men and women participated. GENDER, YOUTH, CAPACITY DEVELOPMENT AND CLIMATE CHANGE Applying incentives to support farmers adopting agroecology practices, such as Bokashi, leads to several expected positive outcomes: improved soil health, productivity, yields, and income [1,3,4, 5,6], as well as Bokashi’s use in home gardens. A follow-up survey will be conducted to assess the adoption and impact of organic fertilizer on smallholders’ production systems and livelihoods. Beyond the economic and environmental benefits, the training should also empower producers by increasing their knowledge and skills, enabling them to make more informed decisions about their farming practices. This empowerment coupled with strengthened community collaboration will foster a sense of shared purpose and community resilience.
