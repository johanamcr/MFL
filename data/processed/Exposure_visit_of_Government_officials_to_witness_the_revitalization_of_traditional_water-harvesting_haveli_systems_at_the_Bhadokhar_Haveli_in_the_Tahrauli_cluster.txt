1 1. Introduction 1.1 Bundelkhand Context The Bundelkhand region of Central India faces severe water scarcity, land degradation, and socio-economic challenges. The region is characterized by hard rock geology with poor aquifer storage capacity, making groundwater recharge difficult. Climatic analysis reveals a disturbing trend: annual average rainfall has declined from 1000 mm (1950) to 750 mm (2017), coupled with frequent dry spells during the monsoon. Consequently, agricultural productivity remains low (0.5 to 1.5 t ha-1), and most areas are limited to a single crop under rainfed conditions. 1.2 Need for Revitalization The biophysical stress has triggered severe social consequences, including large-scale out-migration and Anna Pratha (the abandoning of cattle due to fodder shortages). Crucially, the traditional Haveli water-harvesting systems which once ensured water security have become non-functional over the last three decades due to weak institutional support. Revitalizing these traditional systems is essential to address the region's declining groundwater levels and to support the livelihoods of the rural population. 1.3 Objectives of the Exposure Visit The primary objective of the visit was to demonstrate the efficacy of science-led interventions in restoring degraded agro-ecosystems. The visit aimed to: • To provide high-level policymakers with a firsthand view of a scalable approach that links water resource management, land rejuvenation, and agricultural livelihoods in a climate-stressed region. • To present the technical and social mechanisms behind the successful revitalization of traditional Haveli systems at Bhadokhar, highlighting their role in groundwater recharge and flood management. • To allow delegates to observe the impact of integrated natural resource management strategies including soil conservation, crop diversification, and agroforestry on building community resilience. • To facilitate knowledge exchange among State Level Nodal Agencies (SLNAs) and watershed officers from various states (Rajasthan, Jharkhand, Maharashtra, etc.) regarding the potential replication of these interventions in their respective regions.

Visit Overview The exposure visit hosted two distinct high-profile delegations, representing both the central policy-making level and state-level implementation agencies. On October 8, 2025, the site was visited by senior leadership from the Department of Land Resources (DoLR), Ministry of Rural Development, Government of India: Shri Nitin Khade, IAS, Joint Secretary (Watershed Management) and Dr. C.P. Reddy, Senior Additional Commissioner (WD). The delegation visited the site to witness the implementation of the Bundelkhand Model. During the visit, Shri Nitin Khade observed the ongoing interventions and expressed high satisfaction with the quality of work and the scientific approach applied by ICRISAT. He identified it as a leading model for landscape rejuvenation, noting that scaling up such science-led interventions is the need of the hour for the country. Significantly, the visit highlighted the potential for national policy impact. Acknowledging ICRISAT’s role as a key consortium partner with the National Rainfed Area Authority (NRAA) in framing technical guidelines for Land Resource Inventory (LRI) and Hydrology, Shri Khade emphasized that the methodologies observed in Tahrauli should be integrated into the upcoming PMKSY WDC 3.0 guidelines. He suggested that adopting these practices could drive meaningful policy changes in watershed management nationwide. Encouraged by the success of the project, he explicitly advised State Level Nodal Agencies (SLNAs) across India to visit the site and replicate these learnings. Following the recommendation of the DoLR, a high-level delegation comprising Chief Executive Officers (CEOs) and senior watershed officers from six states Rajasthan, Jharkhand, Maharashtra, Goa, Tamil Nadu, and Madhya Pradesh visited the Tahrauli cluster from October 24–29, 2025. This visit allowed state administrators to witness firsthand how integrated watershed interventions can transform degraded landscapes into productive, climate-resilient ecosystems. The delegates observed the practical application of: Science-based planning using Land Resource Inventory (LRI); Digital tools for real- time monitoring; Participatory governance in managing rainwater harvesting and soil conservation. The delegation was impressed by the tangible outcomes, such as enhanced groundwater recharge leading to crop diversification and improved nutritional security.

4 3. Field Observations The delegation visited the Bhadokhar Haveli to witness the revitalization of a traditional water harvesting system that had been defunct for decades. Officials inspected key technical interventions, including strengthened earthen embankments and a newly constructed masonry structure designed to regulate excess runoff. Visiting in October, the delegation observed the Haveli holding a significant volume of monsoon water, providing visual proof of its storage capacity. The site demonstrated immediate hydrological impacts, with data confirming raised groundwater levels in nearby wells and sufficient residual soil moisture to support the upcoming Rabi cultivation with minimal irrigation. The joint secretory also opened the Bhadokhar haveli system outlet followed by traditional ceremony to let the stored water out of the haveli system as the farmer will take the next crop. 4. Stakeholder Interactions The visit facilitated direct dialogue between the officials and the local farming community, who highlighted the project's transformative impact on their livelihoods. Beneficiaries shared testimonials on how the restored Haveli secured a second crop season, mitigating the risks of dry spells and migration. The delegation also engaged with the community user group and the scientific team, observing how the Bundelkhand Model successfully fuses science-based monitoring with participatory governance to ensure the long-term maintenance and sustainability of community assets. 5. Key Takeaways The exposure visit highlighted three critical pillars of the Bundelkhand Model that drive its success: • The use of Land Resource Inventory (LRI) and hydrological monitoring ensures that interventions are location-specific and technically sound, rather than a one-size-fits-all approach. • The Bhadokhar Haveli demonstrates that revitalizing ancient systems is often more sustainable and cost-effective. • The successful partnership between scientific institutions (ICRISAT), government bodies (DoLR, GoUP), and the community proves that this model is ripe for scaling across similar agro ecologies in India.

5 6. Conclusions The exposure visit to the Tahrauli cluster has successfully positioned the Bhadokhar Haveli revitalization as a benchmark for landscape restoration in Central India. By witnessing the tangible transformation of degraded lands into productive assets, the visiting policymakers have validated the efficacy of the Bundelkhand Model. The endorsement from the Department of Land Resources and the interest from six state governments signal a strong potential for this science-led, community-centric approach to reshape watershed management policies across the country.
