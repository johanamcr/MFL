Summary Here we report on the adoption of Urochloa hybrids – improved forage grasses – by farmers in the SAAF focal countries of Cambodia, Colombia, Kenya, Malawi, Malaysia, Uganda, Vietnam, and Zambia in 2024. The number of adopters was estimated based on the area sown, average farm size, and household size. The total number of people living in newly adopting households in 2024 is estimated at 106,561, comprising 4,319 in Cambodia, 5,503 in Colombia, 9,287 in Kenya, 2,464 in Malawi, 27,607 in Malaysia, 54,712 in Uganda, 1,135 in Vietnam, and 1,534 in Zambia. Support for the distribution of Urochloa hybrids was provided through the SAAF program. Consistent with previous years, we are using the year before the reporting year as we receive seed sales data from our private partner with a 1-year lag. Background The growing global demand for animal-sourced foods (ASF) is placing increasing pressure on livestock systems, particularly in tropical regions where climate change, land degradation, and poor feed quality constrain productivity. Addressing these challenges requires practical, science-based solutions that enhance livestock performance while minimizing environmental impacts. Improved forage technologies represent a key part of the solution. Among these, Urochloa (syn. Brachiaria) hybrids – co-developed by the Alliance of Bioversity International-CIAT and Grupo Papalotla – offer resilient, high- quality feed options for livestock producers. Since the release of the first hybrid, Mulato, in 2001, Urochloa hybrids have expanded rapidly. By 2024, close to 2 million hectares of Urochloa hybrid pastures have been established across >70 countries, benefiting over 1.7 million farmers and almost 13 million members of farm households (Burkart 2025). Adoption has been highest in Latin America, which accounts for approximately 92% of the total planted area (Burkart 2025). The greatest impacts have been observed among smallholder farming communities, who represent 72% of all adopters – over 1.2 million people (Burkart 2025). By alleviating feed shortages and

Although women currently represent only 16% of adopters, their participation in Urochloa-based production systems has contributed to greater economic independence and enhanced decision-making power within households (Burkart et al. 2025; Burkart 2025). Beyond income gains, adoption of Urochloa hybrids has strengthened food security in many local communities (Flórez et al. 2024, Junca Paredes et al. 2023, Mwendia et al. 2022, Fuglie et al. 2021). More productive livestock systems increase the availability of milk (Mwendia et al. 2022; Gonzalez Quintero et al. 2024) and meat (Sandoval et al. 2023, Fuglie et al. 2021, Enciso et al. 2019), helping to address malnutrition, particularly in food- insecure regions (Junca Paredes et al. 2023, Schiek et al. 2018). The CGIAR Sustainable Animal and Aquatic Foods (SAAF) Science Program has played a central role in scaling Urochloa hybrid forages, supporting adoption across 8,638 hectares. These efforts have directly benefited 20,506 livestock producers, including 3,938 women, and reached an estimated 106,561 farm household members across 8 SAAF countries (Burkart 2025). Partners Grupo Papalotla: Supports innovation, development, dissemination, and scaling of Urochloa hybrids globally. Outcomes and supporting evidence Land-use changes Table 1 presents a detailed breakdown of the area planted with Urochloa hybrids across the eight SAAF countries, distinguishing between new areas established in 2024 and the cumulative area since first establishment. Not all Urochloa hybrids observed on farmers’ fields originate from purchased seed; many farmers exchange, sell, or distribute vegetative planting material in the form of splits. For Latin America, Holmann et al. (2004) suggest a split-to-seed ratio of 0.15:1. For Asia and Africa, based on expert consultation, we applied an estimated split-to-seed ratio of 3:1. In 2024, new adoption in the SAAF countries represented 6.1% of total global adoption in the same year (140,823 ha) and 7.1% of the cumulative adoption within the SAAF countries (120,895 ha) (Burkart 2025). These forages help reduce soil erosion, enhance soil carbon sequestration, and lower greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. In Latin America, adoption of Urochloa hybrids is estimated to have avoided between 27 and 64 megatons of CO₂-equivalent emissions, assuming that adopted hybrids either (a) function as improved pastures partially replacing native pastures or (b) are implemented within silvopastoral systems (Burkart 2025). For Colombia – the only SAAF country in the Americas – estimated GHG emission reductions from Urochloa hybrid adoption in 2024 range from 52 to 237 thousand tons of CO₂-eq, depending on the replacement scenario (30%, 50%, or 70%) and whether hybrids were established as monocultures (lower savings) or within silvopastoral systems (higher savings) (Burkart 2025). These reductions are equivalent to the emissions of approximately 1,140–5,161 average passenger vehicles over a 10-year period, corresponding to about 0.001– 0.005% of all registered automobiles in the United States in 2020 (United States Environmental Protection Agency 2022, U.S. Department of Transportation 2023). Next steps While Latin America has well-established markets for Urochloa hybrids, they remain emerging across Africa and Asia (Burkart 2025). Nevertheless, adoption data from the last years shows a growth trend in various of the African and Asian SAAF countries, highlighting potential for broader scaling (Burkart 2025). This was analyzed through several studies conducted under SAAF and B4T, as well as the previous CGIAR Initiatives. To close existing ruminant feed gaps across Sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia, large-scale adoption of improved forages is required. Across 16 Sub-Saharan African countries, improved forages could be scaled over more than 3.2 million hectares within a 10-year period, benefiting over 2.6 million farmers (Burkart & Mwendia 2024; Burkart et al. 2026). Similarly, in Southeast Asia, closing the ruminant feed gap across five countries would require scaling improved forages over more than 314,000 hectares, involving approximately 400,000 adopters (Burkart et al. 2025). In both regions, Urochloa hybrids could potentially accounting for 35% of this area. In East Africa alone, the potential exists to expand Urochloa hybrid adoption by more than 400,000 additional hectares, creating an annual seed market valued at over US$70 million (Junca Paredes et al. 2023). This substantial potential has also been recognized by the private sector. Our scaling partner, Grupo Papalotla, has established an East Africa office in Kenya to coordinate regional seed system development, covering both East Africa and Southeast Asia (Semillas Papalotla 2025; Papalotla Africa 2025). In parallel, Sumitomo Corporation of Americas (SCOA) and Grupo Papalotla have formed a strategic partnership, including an equity

Leveraging CIAT- licensed hybrid pasture seeds, the collaboration aims to support pasture restoration, enhance livestock productivity, and reduce greenhouse gas emissions – initially in Brazil, with planned expansion across tropical regions (Feed & Additive 2025). Building on demonstrated adoption trends and growing private-sector engagement, next steps should focus on strengthening regional seed systems, prioritizing high-potential countries for targeted scaling, expanding farmer capacity and demand creation, and deepening value-chain partnerships (Florez et al. 2024). Aligning these efforts with national feed, pasture restoration, and climate objectives – while continuing to generate robust impact evidence – will be critical to achieving large-scale adoption of Urochloa hybrids across Africa and Southeast Asia (Burkart & Mwendia 2024; Burkart et al. 2025; Burkart et al. 2026; Florez et al. 2024; Junca Paredes et al. 2023).
