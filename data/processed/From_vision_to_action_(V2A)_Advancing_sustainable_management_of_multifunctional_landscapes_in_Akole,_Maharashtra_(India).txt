Introduction | Page 3 of 13 CGIAR Introduction Vision to Action in the context of Multifunctional landscapes The Vision-to-Action (V2A) approach was developed to support inclusive stakeholder conversations to create a shared vision for a desirable future, develop holistic and medium- term transition pathways towards sustainable living landscapes, and define realistic, collectively agreed-upon concrete action plans that outline the behavior changes required to transition from their current situation to the envisioned future. It is designed to foster action that is multi-actor (e.g., producers, traders, enterprises, consumers, policymakers), multi- dimensional (e.g., agricultural, environmental, economic, social), and multi-scalar (e.g., farm, landscape, system) to realize sustainable food system transformation. This method structures conversations to allow stakeholders to discuss and agree about who, what, when and where to engage in the sought transformation processes. The V2A process and results also point to opportunities and entry points for responsive external support including by research-in-development partners such as the CGIAR Science Program on Multifunctional Landscapes. Overview of Akole The Akole cluster is a multifunctional landscape located in western Ahilyanagar district of Maharashtra, within the northern Western Ghats - one of India’s globally recognized biodiversity hotspots and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The landscape lies in the upper Pravara river basin, a major tributary of the Godavari River. It consists of two sub-clusters: the high-, mid-, and low-elevation villages of Kalsubai, and the foothill plains of Igatpuri. The terrain is characterized by steep mountains, rugged valleys, forests, rangelands, and cultivated fields. Peaks such as Kalsubai (1,646 m) and Harishchandragad (1,424 m) define the region’s topography, with forest cover reaching 50% in the hilly zones. Ecologically, Akole supports deciduous and semi-evergreen forests rich in native species like Terminalia chebula, Terminalia bellirica, and diverse grasses. It is home to wildlife including sambar, chital, leopard, wolf, tiger, Indian giant squirrel, and 140+ bird species, protected partly by the Kalsubai–Harishchandragad and Rehekuri Sanctuaries. Despite substantial water resources such as rivers, reservoirs such as Bhandardara (Arthur Lake), and groundwater, availability is constrained by high soil infiltration, erratic rainfall, and degradation. Soil erosion affects long-term agricultural sustainability and productivity of nearly three-quarters of households. Moreover, deforestation is rising due to fuelwood demand and land-use change. The climate is tropical wet–dry, with hot summers, cool winters, and monsoon rains concentrated between June and September. Low-lying Akole receives nearly 509 mm annually, while high-elevation zones like Ghatghar record 2,000–3,000 mm. Climate variability in terms of delayed monsoons, intense rainfall bursts, long dry spells have increased crop damage, erosion, and pest incidence in the landscape. The Akole cluster’s population is about 15,000 across ten villages, with most households depending on agriculture; wage labor; seasonally migrating work; and livestock, especially Dangi cattle, goats, and poultry. Land use reflects its multifunctional character with 46% cropland, 40% rangeland, 7% forest, 6% water bodies, and 3% settlements. Major crops include rice, millets, wheat, pulses, and oilseeds.

CGIAR Introduction | Page 4 of 13 From Vision to Action: The Akole workshop Purpose and objectives The V2A workshop held in Akole (landscape) on 8th August 2025 was designed to formulate a shared vision, embracing diverse land use types and considering broader benefits provided by the landscape. This workshop brought together system actors to jointly imagine the future they want to see for the landscape, fifteen years from now, and identify practical steps to move in that direction. It offered an inclusive platform for all stakeholders to share aspirations, align efforts, and co-develop locally grounded priorities. The approach emphasized organic visioning, context awareness, and early action planning, while remaining flexible to accommodate stakeholder availability and dynamics on the ground. Overview of sessions The workshop was designed as an immersive, one-day participatory exercise to encourage multi-stakeholder dialogue and action planning. It followed a carefully sequenced structure combining plenary inputs, creative visualization, and interactive group activities. Introduction | Page 5 of 13 CGIAR The workshop was designed as an immersive, one-day participatory exercise to encourage multi-stakeholder dialogue and action planning. It followed a carefully sequenced structure combining plenary inputs, creative visualization, and interactive group activities. The opening plenary set the stage by outlining the objectives of the CGIAR Multifunctional Landscapes program, revisiting the collective journey in Akole as part of the Nature-Positive Solutions initiative of the CGIAR and reinforcing the purpose of the V2A process in advancing sustainable development. Informed consent was obtained from all participants to permit the audio and video recording of the workshop as well as the use of pictures in future presentations and publications. This was followed by a visioning exercise wherein participants were invited to imagine Akole fifteen years into the future, envisioning what the landscape would look like if it had flourished ecologically, socially, and economically. Participants shared key elements of their shared vision which has been consolidated into a collective vision statement. This session also included role- play in a “press conference” format where participants from each group assumed the roles of journalists from a documentary team and system actors who reflected on their achievements around enhancing multiple landscape functions and providing diverse benefits to a wide range of stakeholders. In the afternoon sessions, participants undertook a stakeholder mapping exercise, identifying actors who can play a significant role in shaping the future of the Akole landscape aligned with the joint vision. This activity was followed by a deeper reflection on actor relations and exploration of dynamics of power, relationships, and collaboration in the system. The workshop concluded with a reflection circle, where participants shared personal takeaways and agreed on the next steps and follow-up mechanisms. Participant and stakeholder representation The workshop brought together a diverse mix of participants representing multiple sectors and stakeholder groups engaged in the Akole landscape. These included male and female farmers; Panchayati Raj Institution (PRI) representatives, Civil Society Organizations (CSOs) such as BAIF Development Research Foundation (BAIF) and Rural Education and Development (READ) India; research institutions like Indian Council of Agricultural Research (ICAR), ICAR- National Institute of Abiotic Stress Management (NIASM), Mahatma Phule Krishi Vidyapeeth Rahuri (MPKV- Rahuri) and Sangamner College; ASK Foundation- a Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) initiative; and government official (Director of Skill Development Centre, Nashik). The presence of these diverse institutions alongside community members and CSOs created a rich multi-actor dialogue which is an essential foundation for co-developing integrated pathways towards the shared landscape vision. This diversity reflected the workshop’s intent to ensure inclusiveness across gender, age, and institutional roles, fostering a holistic understanding of the landscape’s challenges and opportunities.

CGIAR Introduction | Page 6 of 13 Figure 2: Participants at V2A Workshop, Shirdi Reflections from the group work Activity 1: Visioning The purpose of this activity was to bring stakeholders together to create a shared vision for the future. It constitutes a major milestone in the development of a collective action plan. All landscape actors present in the workshop were invited to collectively imagine and formulate a multi-dimensional vision of a desirable future for both the landscape and all who depend on it, looking 15 years ahead. They were encouraged to reflect on the diverse benefits experienced by different groups, as well as the burdens that some may bear because of others’ actions. Participants were individually encouraged to articulate an aspirational vision. They were asked: What would the landscape look like if it were shaped by your highest aspirations? By engaging participants in the collective development of this vision, the process nurtures a shared sense of purpose and motivation, paving the way for transformative change. It also fosters essential group qualities such as cohesion and a unified sense of direction. Participants were divided into five groups. The groups were formed based on stakeholder type such that the group composition allowed participants to share their perspectives openly and encourage active participation. Group 1 included men and women farmers, educators and NGO representative; Group 2 brought together men farmers who were also members of village watershed committee together with PRI representatives; Group 3 consisted men and women farmers including youth; Group 4 included officers from government departments and researchers; and Group 5 represented civil society organizations. The facilitator invited participants to imagine traveling 15 years into the future. A documentary team has arrived in the landscape to interview various stakeholders because the area has gained recognition for its remarkable progress - particularly for how its actors have successfully enhanced multiple landscape functions and provided diverse benefits to a wide range of groups. Participants were encouraged to reflect on what messages they would want to share with the documentary team about these achievements. The groups brainstormed for 20 minutes and then wrote short statements on moderation cards which are potential elements of

The facilitator ensured that all ‘journalists’ and ‘system actors’ have equal opportunity to ask questions and answer questions, respectively. The vision articulated by Akole’s landscape actors imagines a cluster that, by 2040, emerges as a vibrant, ecologically balanced, and self-driven landscape shaped entirely by collective vision, community leadership, and a deep commitment to conservation. Across the region, biodiversity flourishes as forests regenerate, soils are restored, and rivers flow clean due to decades of sustained watershed management and careful management of natural resources.

CGIAR Introduction | Page 8 of 13 Native species thrive, medicinal plants are conserved, grasslands are revitalized, and wildlife has returned to restored habitats, reflecting a community-wide shift toward responsible and conservation-minded behavior. Sustainable and climate resilient agriculture forms the backbone of Akole’s rural economy. Villages have transitioned fully to organic and climate smart farming supported by improved soil fertility, reduced carbon emissions, and the adoption of carbon farming technologies. Organic fertilizer production, responsible bioprospecting, and stronger value chains around traditional rice, Karvand (Conkerberry), Jatropha, and other local products create diversified green livelihoods. The closure of chemical input shops and the rise of high yield sustainable agriculture demonstrate strong local resolve to protect the land. Water security is a defining achievement. Community led watershed management, soil conservation, and the revival of rivers have ensured clean drinking water in every village, freeing communities from dependency on external systems. Water availability supports widespread organic farming, healthier ecosystems, and improved community well-being. Akole’s economy is enriched by eco-tourism, agro-tourism, and a variety of nature-based enterprises that attract visitors from major cities seeking clean air, peaceful surroundings, and tribal and eco-friendly tourism experiences. Women’s leadership shines through seed preservation, knowledge sharing, and active roles in local governance, contributing to a culture of empowerment and environmental consciousness. By 2040, Akole stands as a model of resilience, self-reliance, and cultural pride. Scientific approaches blend seamlessly with traditional knowledge, enabling communities to respond effectively to climate variability. The landscape embodies the principle that collective action, shared purpose, and strong partnerships can transform any landscape. Activity 2: Stakeholder mapping The next activity was designed to better understand which actors play a vital role in the landscape and can play a significant role in moving towards the joint vision for Akole’s future. The same groups as for the visioning reflected for 20 minutes on the important stakeholders and then reported back in plenary. Introduction | Page 9 of 13 CGIAR • Politicians • Farmer Producer Organizations (FPOs) Activity 3: Reflection on actor relations The next activity in the workshop aimed to dive deeper into the relationship between actors in relation to the landscape. This was facilitated through an embodiment practice, more specifically an organizational constellation, allowing participants to intuitively experience the current state of the social system. This practice aims to help participants reflect on potential blind spots and sense a desired future. It encourages innovative thinking regarding actor relations and power dynamics. For the activity, participants were asked to sit in a large circle with a big open space in the middle. Based on the earlier stakeholder mapping activity, the participants prioritized the ten most important actors. The key actors identified by the participants, in no particular order, were: • Student • Politician • Progressive farmer • Research organization • Local resident • Funding agency • Government department • PRI representative • SHG/FPO member • NGO representative The facilitator then explained that the participants shall adopt the roles of these ten shortlisted system actors. As the facilitator called out one of these ten actors, starting with the more important ones, one person from the group was invited to volunteer to play this actor. It was emphasized that the person playing the role of an actor should not be closely affiliated with this actor in real life (e.g., a representative of a ministry should not play the role of a minister). Once a volunteer was identified, the facilitator gave the nametag with the actor’s name to the person. The person was asked to sense into this role by moving around and finding a place in the room and creating a shape or gesture that intuitively embodies the actor’s experience within the system. Once they found a place in the room and established their gesture, they were asked to remain in the assumed gesture and position. Thereafter the second actor was invited and the same procedure repeated. As the second actor entered the open space, they could (but did not have to) relate to the first actor. Once the second actor found their position, the process repeated until all ten actors were in the open space in the middle. Once all participants had entered the space, the facilitator invited them to adjust their positions and shapes or gestures to intuitively represent the system's actors. This outcome is referred to as Sculpture 1 (as seen in Figure 4). The actors shared their thoughts or feelings at this point as described below: • Funding agency: Wanted funds to directly reach the poorest and most vulnerable. • Research organization: Emphasized the importance of both applied and foundational research that moves between lab and field. Research must be explained simply to students, teachers, farmers, and policymakers; there is the need to convince politicians of the relevance of research.

Introduction | Page 11 of 13 CGIAR The transition from Sculpture 1 to Sculpture 2 reflects a gradual shift from a fragmented and hierarchical system toward one that is more collaborative and grounded. It illustrated that collective action is essential for regional development and achieving the 2040 vision. However, it was also observed in Sculpture 2 that while most actors came together and held hands to gesture their combined efforts and willingness to collaborate to achieve a common goal (Figure 5), the politician and Government department representative did not join the other actors and gestured they would like to stay away when they were invited to join. The student representative stood close to the circle where other actors came together but did not join hands with them. Once the movements came to a stop again, actors were asked to say a few words that came intuitively to their minds. The following points describe their intuitive reflections: • Progressive farmer: “I believe our country is an agricultural country. If farmers and researchers come together, and if all other actors join us, the country will prosper. Unfortunately, we saw how government departments and politicians stepped aside when unity was needed.” • Research organization: “I want to emphasize the need for alignment between scientific knowledge and governance. If science and government come together, then the paths of prosperity will open for all.” • Funding agency: “If we consider our country our own and take responsibility and do something for it, and if we keep nature in good condition, then the country will definitely improve, and we will all be happy.” • NGO representative: “For rural development, there is a need for all actors to come together. We need collaboration across all levels of governance so that the time required for all-round development is reduced.” • Local resident: “If farmers are prosperous, then everyone will be prosperous.” After another short pause, the people in the middle were asked to leave their role and go back to their chairs. Following the embodiment activity, participants were invited to share what they saw and felt in their assigned roles. Several noted a clear and persistent disconnect between the administration, politicians, and the broader community. One participant expressed concern that the administration and funding agencies seemed distant from the people they were meant to serve, and that such a gap should not exist. The actors’ movement towards Sculpture 2 was interpreted as a symbolic alignment of shared purpose, demonstrating that unity among actors is the foundation upon which real and sustained development becomes possible. Actors observed that while stakeholders initially appeared disconnected and uncertain about their roles, the process of guided reflection enabled them to recognize their place within the system. As they adjusted their positions, they began to see where meaningful development could emerge, thus reiterating the importance of collective visioning. CGIAR Introduction | Page 12 of 13 Participants also recognized that research, once seen as an isolated activity, is in fact linked to every actor in the system and must be communicated simply and inclusively. The idea of collective research and co-creation emerged as an important insight. The reflections concluded with a reminder that development is rooted in unity. Participants emphasized that since India mostly lives in its villages, strong and functional local governance and coordination across institutions are essential for progress. Way forward and closure of workshop The closing remarks from the facilitator further reinforced the spirit of shared responsibility and action. The group was reminded that the purpose of the exercise was not merely to envision change, but to inspire concrete action that continues beyond the workshop and throughout the year. It was emphasized that the responsibility for realizing the vision lies primarily with local actors and institutions in Akole, while development partners and supporting organizations must continue to collaborate and stand alongside them. Together, these reflections marked a fitting close to the workshop, reaffirming that the vision for 2040 is not only aspirational but also actionable. It can be realized through inclusive participation, institutional convergence, and sustained local leadership across the landscape.
