Executive Summary Ethiopia, the largest wheat producer in sub-Saharan Africa, relies heavily on wheat for both household income and food security. In the Dogoyena district in the southern part of the country, there is significant potential to advance agroecological practices within the wheat value chain. While most smallholders primarily depend on wheat for their livelihoods, many are beginning to diversify their crops as part of broader economic diversification efforts. This shift is occurring despite government initiatives promoting wheat intensification to meet growing national and international demand. Producers Doyogena is a critical project area for the Private Sector Incentives and Investments (PSii) under the TRANSITIONS program due to it has conditions that favor the cultivation of wheat, which is predominantly cultivated by smallholder producers in the district. This study focuses on four wheat clusters in the Dogoyena district: Tumma, Adoye, Selam, and Telle. This study is crucial as it will help identify the most effective incentives and investment opportunities within wheat clusters, considering the social and economic local contexts of the key stakeholders in the Dogoyena district. This report is part of the PSii project and provides an in-depth analysis of three cooperative business models in four wheat clusters, as well as of agroecological practices in Ethiopia. Utilizing focus group discussions and the Business Model Canvas tool, the study offers a comprehensive examination of wheat production strategies and their alignment with sustainable practices. The analysis of farmer cooperatives from four wheat farming clusters (Tumma, Adoye, Selam, and Telle) reveals distinct approaches to wheat production. Amecho Seed Multiplication cooperative (Adoyo cluster) and the Serara producer cooperative (Telle and Tumma cluster) focus on high- quality production and strong customer relationships, investing significantly in labor, inputs, and credit. Angacha producers Cooperative (Selam cluster) integrates resource sharing through partnerships while Serara emphasizes waste reduction and manages costs related to interest rates, storage, and transportation. The findings on agroecological practices show that producers have low engagement in market connectivity and integration, indicating a need for improved market linkages and cooperative development. Knowledge sharing and multi-stakeholder participation are also insufficient, emphasizing the need for better extension services and collaborative learning. Although some agroecological practices are adopted, there is significant potential for improvement in soil health, biodiversity, and fairness in income distribution. Encouraging practices like cover cropping, reduced tillage, and integrated pest management can enhance sustainability and resilience. This report analyzes the opportunities for improvement and appropriate investments and incentives to promote and support the transition towards more sustainable practices in the study area.

9 Pathways to sustainable wheat business models: Exploring agroecological practices and investment opportunities in Doyogena, Ethiopia Wheat is one of the earliest domesticated crops and remains a crucial component of global food production. As a key source nutrient, it plays an essential role in addressing food security and nutrition challenges, particularly in regions with limited dietary diversity. In 2018, wheat cultivation covered approximately 217 million hectares, surpassing all other crops in land area and underscoring its significance in agricultural systems worldwide (Erenstein et al., 2022). Wheat’s adaptability to different climates and its resilience to variable weather conditions make it a vital component of food systems in both developing and developed countries. This is especially true for regions striving to meet growing food demands. In Africa, the significance of wheat is increasing, driven by population growth, urbanization, dietary shifts, and the need to address food security. The continent’s diverse agroecological zones, coupled with various initiatives aimed at boosting productivity to address food security challenges, offer vast potential for expanding wheat production, reinforcing its role in the global food supply. Despite its potential, Africa faces challenges such as climate change, limited access to quality seeds, and inadequate infrastructure. In Ethiopia, wheat is a vital staple food crop, essential for both nourishment and income for millions of smallholder producers. However, the sector faces several challenges such as climate change and soil degradation. These issues are often exacerbated by existing production practices. Transitioning to agroecological and resilient production systems is essential for ensuring food security for local communities and climate resilience. For this reason, the TRANSITIONS program seeks to align policy, investment, and technical support to enable climate- informed agroecological transitions for producers in low and middle-income countries. The program promotes developing and adopting holistic metrics for food and agricultural systems’ performance, inclusive digital tools, and transparent private-sector engagement to foster incentives and leverage investment. The Private Sector Incentives and Investments (PSii) project contributes to the broader TRANSITIONS program objectives. This report is part of Component 1 of the PSii project. The primary objective of this research is to analyze the business model structures of wheat clusters and the agroecological practices within these systems using the canvas business model methodology, a tool used to analyze and design a business model that consists of a matrix with nine blocks representing key aspects of the business structure. By providing insights into these practices and identifying potential incentives, the research objective is providing evidence to stakeholders in the wheat clusters, specifically in the input and production stages, on gaps and aims to discover opportunities for improvement, ultimately fostering a more sustainable and resilient wheat sector. The report is organized into five sections. The first section provides a state-of-the-art overview of the wheat sector, focusing on wheat production in Ethiopia and the specific local context of Doyogena. The methodology section presents the tools used to conduct the research, including the Business Model Canvas and a self-evaluation tool to assess agroecological practices. The results section analyzes business improvement opportunities through an agroecological approach and evaluates the adoption of sustainable practices alongside producer performance. Finally, the conclusion and recommendations section summarize the key findings, highlighting opportunities for investment and related incentives. Bioversity-CIAT, Ethiopia Introduction

State of Art in the Wheat Sector 1.1 Wheat in Ethiopia Wheat is a crucial staple food crop in Ethiopia (Hodson et al., 2020; Melese et al., 2022), serving as both a vital source of nourishment and income for millions of smallholder producers. As the largest producer of wheat in sub-Saharan Africa since 2005, Ethiopia annually cultivates wheat on approximately 1.6 to 1.8 million hectares, supporting around 5 million farming households (Hodson et al., 2020). Wheat’s significance is underscored by its contribution to the agricultural sector and household food security (Anteneh et al., 2020). The rainfed wheat production is predominantly carried out during the main rainy season in Ethiopia (June to October) in the highlands of the country, while irrigated wheat production is carried out from November to April in the lowlands along the Awash, Wabe Shebele, and Omo River basins (Tadesse et al., 2022) Table 1 presents the annual wheat production, export, and import volumes in Ethiopia from 2018 to 2022. As can be seen, Ethiopia’s wheat production has steadily increased from 4,838 thousand tons in 2018 to a peak of 5,808 thousand tons in 2021, before slightly decreasing to 5,800 thousand tons in 2022. Despite the overall growth in production, wheat exports have remained minimal, with the highest export volume recorded in 2022 at just 11 thousand tons. This indicates that Ethiopia primarily produces wheat for domestic consumption rather than for export. On the other hand, wheat imports varied, with the lowest volume in 2020 at 1,208 thousand tons and the highest in 2021 at 2,052 thousand tons, which corresponds to the highest production year. Interestingly, while production was almost stable in 2022 compared to 2021, imports decreased to 1,510 thousand tons. Wheat is one of the world’s oldest and most widely cultivated food crops. Along with rice and maize, it is one of the three staple cereals that form the foundation of the global human diet. These cereals account for nearly half of the world’s food calories and two-fifths of the world’s protein intake. Wheat plays a vital role in ensuring global food and nutritional security. By 2018, wheat was grown on approximately 217 million hectares of land globally, making it the most widely cultivated crop in the world (Erenstein et al., 2022). Global per capita annual consumption of wheat is 65.6 kg (Erenstein et al., 2022), contributing over one- third to global per capita annual cereal consumption (175 kg). Wheat is the second most consumed cereal, behind rice, which has a per capita consumption of 81 kg (Erenstein et al., 2022). Given wheat’s central role in the global food system, any disruptions to major wheat-producing regions, such as droughts, wars, or other crises, can have far-reaching effects on global food security and supply chains. Global wheat production is concentrated in a few key countries. According to the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAOSTAT, 2014), leading wheat producers are China (17% of global production), India (12.5%), Russia (8.4%), the United States (8.4%), and France (8.4%). The conflict between Russia and Ukraine, both significant wheat producers, has caused significant disruptions to the global wheat market, highlighting the vulnerability of the global food system to geopolitical events (Rastogi & Ang, 2022). The global significance of wheat as a staple food crop makes its production critical to food security worldwide. 11 Pathways to sustainable wheat business models: Exploring agroecological practices and investment opportunities in Doyogena, Ethiopia The rapid increase in wheat production and the continuing demand for imports indicate a transforming wheat sector. Productivity gains have been significant, but Ethiopia still faces challenges in closing the gap between wheat supply and demand. Achieving self-sufficiency in wheat production is a national priority, driven by the need to ensure food security and reduce the country’s dependence on costly wheat imports (Hodson et al., 2020). While wheat production in Ethiopia has great potential, it remains relatively small by global standards, primarily due to the reliance on subsistence farming and rain- fed production systems. Its marketing and production systems are constrained by various factors, including limited infrastructure and the lack of large-scale irrigation (Anteneh et al., 2020). However, there are also opportunities for growth, such as expanding irrigable land, improving infrastructure, and training producers in value-adding activities. 1.2 Local context: Dogoyena The study area, Doyogena District, is situated in the Kembata Zone of Central Ethiopia, approximately 258 km southwest of Addis Ababa, the nation’s capital. The district encompasses a total area of 17,264 hectares, divided into 14 sub-districts. The land use distribution is predominantly agricultural, with about 86% allocated for crop cultivation, 11% covered by forests and bushes, 2% designated as grazing land, and 0.2% classified as degraded land. The altitude of Doyogena District ranges from 1,900 to 2,748 meters above sea level, featuring two primary agroecological zones: the Highlands (Dega), which constitutes 70% of the area, and the Upper Midlands (Woyina dega), which accounts for the remainder 30%. The district experiences a mean annual rainfall between 1,000 and 1,400 mm, with temperatures ranging from 12.6°C to 20°C. Moreover, wheat is the most grown crop next to Enset (Ensete ventricosum) by smallholder producers. The local farming system is characterized by an Enset- cereal-livestock production model. Key crops cultivated in the district include wheat, barley, legumes, and vegetables such as beans and potatoes. Enset, a vital food source, is cultivated by nearly all households, on an average of 0.5 hectares. Livestock production in the area includes cattle, sheep, and poultry. Given that wheat is predominantly cultivated by smallholder producers and the area’s suitability for wheat production, Doyogena is a critical project area for the TRANSITIONS program. Dogoyena district: A key area for agroecological promotion in wheat clusters In the Doyogena district, the agricultural practices among wheat clusters are closely tied to the area’s varying altitudes, each of which presents unique challenges and opportunities for producers. At higher altitudes, a common practice involves rotating wheat with barley during the main rainy season. In the shorter rainy season, producers grow potatoes, which are often paired with cover crops like vetch or lupin. These cover crops serve the dual purpose of feeding livestock and preparing the soil for the next planting season. Producers in these areas also adopt the use of soil bunds, reinforced with desho grass, which stabilizes the bunds and provides livestock feed. (Balcha et al., 2024) In the mid-altitude regions of Doyogena, producers employ a different rotation system, involving wheat, maize, haricot beans, and potatoes. During the short rainy season, fields are often planted with a mixture of vegetables, maize, potatoes, and haricot beans. This intercropping strategy not only reduces the risk of crop loss, but also minimizes nutrient competition by utilizing crops with different root zones, while simultaneously providing households with a diverse and nutritious food supply. However, the practice is somewhat limited by the scarcity of haricot bean seeds, which poses a challenge to implementing crop rotations that include leguminous crops. (Balcha et al., 2024) Building on these localized agricultural practices, the study case engaged cooperatives active within the cluster areas of the intervention zone in the Dogoyena district. Representatives who play a significant role in cooperative activities and decision-making from a number of cooperatives were invited to participate. The sessions included 33 smallholder wheat producers, focusing on their involvement and input. Key cooperatives involved included Angacha farmers’ cooperative provides fertilizer and storage services for their members in Selam Cluster. In addition, the cooperative will supply planting seeds from their previous harvest to their members with less price in case members do not have enough seed for the coming harvesting season. Furthermore, these cooperatives are engaged in income diversifying activities and generate additional benefits to their members. The Amecho Seed Multiplication Cooperative provides improved seeds, storage services, renting out tractors and seed combiners to their members from Adoye Cluster. The Serara farmers’ cooperative provides fertilizer and storage services for their members from Tumma and Telle Clusters. In addition, this cooperative will supply planting seeds from their previous harvest to members at a lower price in case members do not have enough seed for the coming harvesting season. Methodology This study employed a multi-stage approach, integrating both qualitative and quantitative methods to achieve the research objectives. The process was carried out in the following steps: I. Questionnaire customization: The first step involved adapting the questions from the Business Agroecology Criteria Tool (B-ACT) to fit the local context of Doyogena and the wheat cluster farming system. The customized questionnaire was tailored to capture relevant information specific to the study area. II. Cooperative selection: Four wheat cluster areas were identified within the project’s intervention zones, and active cooperatives within these clusters were selected for the study: Angacha Farmers’ Cooperative from Selam Cluster; Amecho Seed Multiplication Cooperative from Adoyo Cluster; and Serara farmers’ Cooperative, representing both Telle and Tumma Clusters. III. Data collection workshop: Representatives from the selected cooperatives were invited to participate in a two-day data collection workshop held in Doyogena. A total of 33 wheat producers, chosen to represent leadership and active members, attended the workshop. The participants were randomly selected from the clusters as follows: 6 from Selam, 8 from Adoyo, 9 from Telle, and 10 from Tumma, with the number of participants adjusted to reflect the size of the respective clusters. These workshops took place during 15th and 16th May. During the workshop, participants engaged in group discussions and applied the Business Model Canvas methodology, guided by a set of structured questions. IV. Individual interviews: To further analyze the adoption and implementation of agroecological practices, one- on-one interviews were conducted with 26 individual wheat producers. This step used the Performance Self-Evaluation Tool for Agroecological Practices to assess the producers’ engagement with agroecological methods. The interviewees were selected from 14 wheat cluster farms across the district, comprising both project intervention clusters (four) and control groups (ten). Two producers were interviewed from each cluster, except for Telle and Tumma, where one producer from each was selected, resulting in a total of 26 participants. Figure 3. Data Collection Steps. 14 | REPORT 3.1. Link Methodology: Business Canvas Model The Canvas methodology, rooted in the Business Model Canvas developed by Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010) offers a streamlined approach to visualizing and designing business models. This methodology facilitates proactive problem-solving by aiding in the early recognition of potential challenges. It shows how each element of a business structure interconnects and contributes to the overall business objective (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010). This study uses the Canvas tool considering the Link methodology toolkit to understand the business model of wheat producers and their current clients to identify gaps and synergies to promote agroecological transitions. Link Methodology allows the analysis of a business from a general perspective to understand how it works and discover new opportunities for innovation. This is based on applying a participatory toolkit that includes the value chain map, Business Model Canvas, new business model, and prototype cycle (Lundy et al., 2014). Using business model analysis in agriculture is increasingly popular, as agricultural businesses face a range of challenges related to sustainability, profitability, and competitiveness. 3.2. Performance Self-Evaluation Tool for Agroecological Practices The Performance Self-Evaluation Tool for Agroecological Practices was created as a means for producers to assess their own involvement in agroecological methods. This tool draws upon well-known frameworks like the Business Agroecology Criteria Tool (B-ACT) and the Tool for Agroecology Performance Evaluation (TAPE), which were developed by Biovision and FAO in 2019 (FAO, 2019). The tool is structured as a detailed questionnaire, comprising agroecological practices that are mapped to 13 core agroecological principles. The questions are tailored to be relevant and easily understood by the respondents. Wheat producers assess their practices using a structured rating scale from 0 to 3, where: 0 indicates no interest or alignment with the practice; 1 signifies the practice has not yet been implemented but is desired; 2 reflects the practice is in place but requires improvement; and 3 represents full adoption with substantial experience in the practice. If a specific practice were not applicable to the organization, participants could mark it as N.A. (not applicable). This detailed rating system facilitated a thorough evaluation of the participants’ engagement in agroecological practices. This tool was employed by Blanco et al. (2024) in Peru for evaluating agroecological practices in the cocoa sector. In this study, the tool was adapted to the context of Ethiopia and wheat production. The adaptation ensured the tool’s relevance and effectiveness in assessing agroecological practices within a different agricultural and cultural setting. 16 | REPORT The customer base of the cooperative is diverse, including work association unions, traders, personal users, retailers, and various associations, with whom the wheat cluster has a medium level of interaction. The cooperative sells its products based on market demand and value, optimizing sales during profitable seasons. This strategy reflects a flexible approach, allowing the cooperative to maximize revenue by aligning sales with market conditions. An estimated profit margin of 20–25% is obtained from sales in cash payments from customers. In addition, the business structure is diverse, adopting a variety of agricultural activities for income diversification through animal husbandry and sales of animal feed promoting a circular economy for their operations. The cooperative revenue is generated from primary product categories like wheat and dench, with profits distributed among association members once or twice yearly. Achieving a profit margin of 7%, the cooperative maintains a modest but stable financial footing. Cost management remains vital, with fixed costs in raw materials and transport, while labor costs vary. There is currently no third-party cost coverage, indicating potential opportunities for partnerships to mitigate expenses. Key resources, such as labor, knowledge, professional support, and infrastructure like warehouses and land, solar energy, and water, are essential for agricultural activities. The cooperative also leverages savings and loans for financial support, while community-based resources, including cultural relationships and networks, enhance their social capital. In this sense, key partners include suppliers of raw materials and partners who provide essential resources and financial loans. The cooperative engages in key activities, such as preparing land and selecting crops. Post-harvest processes, such as drying and cleaning, are crucial. However, traditional collection methods result in a 20% wastage rate, highlighting an area for potential improvement. The cost structure of the cluster includes significant expenses related to labor force and interest rates on loans, as well as fixed costs such as storage and materials, and variable costs like transportation and labor. The cooperative value proposition is centered around high-quality wheat tailored to customer demands, with differentiation through color, amount, and visual characteristics. A key strength is their ability to deliver consistent quality produce, setting them apart from competitors. While 5% of their produce undergoes value- adding processes, such as sorting and packing, they maintain minimal product rejection. The cooperative uses traditional methods, including human resources, cars, carriages, and donkeys, though producers bear transportation costs themselves. Products are stored in community warehouses, individual facilities, or on farms, with the capability to store for 3–5 months, supporting staggered sales and efficient supply management. Communication with customers occurs during harvest and sales seasons through letters, phone calls, and meetings, fostering relationships marked by cooperation and mutual. Pricing is dynamic, determined by market studies and reflecting environmental trade conditions. Financially, loans provide crucial support, though there’s room for expanded financial development. In conclusion, the cooperative demonstrates a well- rounded and dynamic approach to agricultural operations considering an agroecological approach, characterized by its diverse customer base, circular economy practices, adaptable market strategies, and emphasis on environmental impact minimization such as by adopting solar energy. Its revenue streams are diversified through wheat sales, animal husbandry, and supplementary activities, promoting financial stability despite challenges like traditional post-harvest practices and high operational costs. While current operations ensure consistent quality and customer satisfaction, opportunities exist to reduce waste, enhance value- adding processes, and expand third-party collaborations to optimize costs and improve efficiency. These strengths position the cooperative as a reliable player in the regional market while highlighting areas for growth and innovation. The Amecho Seed Multiplication Cooperative The Amecho Seed Multiplication Cooperative serves the Adoye Cluster and consists of 92 producers, predominantly male (72), who cultivate wheat on 60 ha. The cooperative primarily targets retailers as their main customers, along with surrounding producers who purchase seeds. Customers preferences mentioned high- quality, disease-free, dry and clean grains and seeds. Since retailers are interested in purchasing wheat at lower prices, there is a need for competitive pricing strategies. The cooperative is recognized for its focus on quality and consistency, ensuring that customer expectations are met through meticulous production and post- harvest processes. Products are delivered to customers using farm-based methods, with producers bearing transportation costs. The short travel time of 5–10 minutes indicates proximity to customers. Customer relationships are strong, with regular communication, meeting 3–4 times a year and using in-person negotiations to discuss product volume, pricing, and quality. Relationships are described as good, suggesting effective engagement and satisfaction with the cooperative’s offerings. The cooperative achieves an annual sales value reaching around 37,500 Ethiopian birr (ETB) (equivalent to 711.3 USD) and typically the payment is made in cash.

Market Connectivity: A key issue is low engagement in connectivity practices. This lack of integration into market value chains is a major obstacle to building a more sustainable wheat industry. Stronger linkages across the entire value chain – from input provision to product marketing – are needed to help producers access broader markets, secure fair prices, and improve their overall economic resilience. Cooperatives should strengthen market linkages establishing partnerships with flour mills, bakeries, and other potential buyers to secure stable demand and favorable pricing. Establishing formal contracts with these buyers can further ensure reliable market access, improving economic stability for cooperative members and reducing price volatility risks. 2. Research: Doyogena has favorable climatic conditions and an agricultural landscape ideal for wheat production. There is a need to address the unsuitability of existing seed varieties for producing high-demand products. To meet these challenges, cooperatives, research institutes, and government entities must collaborate on prioritizing the adoption and improvement of seed varieties that are climate-smart and resilient in addition to meeting quality standards for commercial bread and cookie production. This will create more resilient wheat production and new market opportunities and ensure that local wheat is suitable for a wider range of products. 3. Private sector: The findings indicate that while producers are not yet engaged in practices such as input reduction, soil health, biodiversity, and synergies, they express interest in adopting them. Partnerships with public institutions already represent an important step forward for the adoption of agroecological practices. Policymakers and stakeholders should prioritize investments in training programs that promote sustainable practices, including organic fertilizer production, intercropping, integrated pest management, crop rotation, etc. The findings reveal that the cooperatives in the study provide essential agricultural services to their members, such as fertilizer supply, storage services, and affordable seed distribution. The cooperatives play a vital role in supporting their members’ agricultural activities, enhancing productivity, and promoting income diversification. However, the analysis also highlights key challenges and areas for improvement to optimize their impact. The cooperatives’ market power is further limited by the lack of access to affordable financial resources. Traditional lending institutions, such as banks and microfinance organizations, often impose high-interest rates and collateral requirements, making it difficult for cooperatives to secure loans. Creating favorable financial conditions would enable cooperatives to invest in essential activities and contribute to agricultural development. Moreover, cooperatives should leverage traditional credit associations (equib), self-help groups (idir), and grants to access financial resources. 4. Infrastructure: The business model canvas analysis shows that investment in technology and seed storage can lead to improvements. However, the transportation infrastructure is a significant barrier. High transportation costs and inadequate infrastructure undermine seed quality and limit market access, especially since most wheat production is concentrated in the wheatbelts of the highland areas in Amhara and Oromia regions. Efficient distribution channels are essential to move wheat from surplus to deficit areas, often hundreds of miles apart. Although the Ethiopian Grain Enterprise controls the supply chain in urban areas, rural transportation remains decentralized, adding complexity to the supply chain (Bergh et al., 2019). Transportation challenges are compounded by the fact that most smallholder producers sell their wheat within local markets (Bergh et al., 2019). Localized sales reflect limited connectivity to broader markets, which restricts economic opportunities and contributes to inefficiencies in the wheat value chain. 5. Innovation: The cooperatives’ engagement in income-diversifying activities contributes to economic stability and resilience for their members. For example, Angacha Farmers’ Cooperative’s diversification efforts have strengthened its bargaining power in setting prices and timing sales. Similarly, Amecho Seed Multiplication Cooperative’s provision of improved seeds, tractor rentals, and storage facilities increases its influence in the market. Nonetheless, these advantages are constrained by systemic challenges, such as limited access to credit, lack of financial incentives, and inadequate market information. These factors hinder the cooperatives’ ability to expand their market reach, particularly in Doyogena district, where wheat varieties produced are considered not suitable for key products like commercial bread and biscuits. 6. Outcomes: This study offers an overview of wheat production and the adoption of agroecological practices among producers in Doyogena, Ethiopia, highlighting the challenges and opportunities in the transition toward sustainable agriculture. Its findings are crucial for informing policymakers and stakeholders, providing guidance for the development of strategies that promote a more resilient and sustainable wheat industry. Focusing on these six key areas will enable policymakers and stakeholders to create an environment that supports the adoption of agroecological practices, ultimately transforming Ethiopia’s wheat sector. Strengthening market connectivity, fostering research and innovation, enhancing private sector engagement, and improving infrastructure will address existing challenges and unlock new opportunities for producers. By promoting sustainable farming techniques, such as integrated pest management, crop diversification, and climate- resilient practices, wheat producers can improve productivity while reducing environmental impacts. Moreover, facilitating access to financial resources, modern technologies, and broader markets will help smallholder farmers increase their economic stability and competitiveness. This integrated approach not only strengthens the resilience and sustainability of the wheat sector but also contributes to national food security, rural livelihoods, and long-term economic growth.
