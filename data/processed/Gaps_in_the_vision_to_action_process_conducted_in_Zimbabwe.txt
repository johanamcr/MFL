Page | ii EXECUTIVE SUMMARY The CGIAR initiative “Transformational Agroecology across Food, Land and Water Systems” works in Zimbabwe's Mbire and Murehwa districts. The initiative supports the transition to environmentally sustainable, productive, and socially just agri-food systems in Zimbabwe. Now in its third year, this report assesses past and ongoing activities to enhance alignment between project activities and community visions. The report is based on qualitative data analysis from document reviews, fieldwork in the two districts, and key informant interviews with current and potential participants. Key findings of the report include: • Most project activities align with farmers’ visions, though gaps remain in all five target areas. There is strong alignment with achieving high levels of crop and livestock production, but gaps in water access and understanding the gradual transition process need to be addressed. Activities align with increasing farmers’ access to functional markets, but issues like market information and overcoming quantity bottlenecks remain. Communities have adopted strategies to manage natural resources, but fires and deforestation persist, and wildlife coexistence in Mbire remains challenging. Communities have some exposure to participation initiatives but need training to enhance active community participation. Partner organisations address gender inequality and GBV, but changing attitudes requires community leader support and more collaboration amongst development actors. • The Initiative's actions are based on two assumptions: knowledge and incentives drive behaviour change. The Behaviour Change Wheel supports these assumptions but highlights that behaviour change is a gradual process requiring time for farmers to test interventions. • The initiative identified 472 stakeholders critical to local food system transitions, grouped into four categories: community, development, governance, and private sector. Community and governance stakeholders were most active, while private sector and development actors were underrepresented. Despite limited engagement, private sector actors provided essential services like machinery, seed, and contract farming arrangements. Moving forward, the Initiative will engage more development and private sector actors to address market access, gender inequality, and GBV. The report's final section outlines output and impact indicators to measure progress toward V2A goals.

Page | 1 1. Introduction Agroecology has been presented as a transformative alternative to the current food system (Caquet et al., 2020). Its 13 guiding principles promote environmental sustainability, productivity, economic benefits, and social justice, focusing more on local ownership and benefits than most conventional agricultural models (Clapp, 2021). Most agroecological interventions target farmers whose operations can benefit from input reduction and recycling and trigger wider biophysical, economic, and social changes (Prost et al., 2023). However, working with farmers alone is not enough. Realizing agroecology’s full transformative potential requires diverse actors in the food system to change or modify their behaviour (Côte et al., 2022). Organizations aiming to promote the widespread adoption of agroecology must address the three sources of behaviour change: capacity, opportunity, and motivation (Michie et al., 2011; Travers et al., 2021). First, capacity is defined as the physical ability and the skills required to change behaviour. Many actors in the food system often lack the technical skills to transition to agroecology. Unfortunately, actors with knowledge, such as extension officers, are too few and often lack the requisite resources to support the other actors in adopting the knowledge- and labour-intensive practices (Antwi-Agyei & Stringer, 2021). Second, the policy environment and market conditions should create a conducive agroecology environment so interested actors can adopt it (Côte et al., 2022). Third, the actors will be motivated to transition to agroecology if they have the requisite capacity and adequate opportunity (Anderson et al., 2019). When these three are in place, interventions can be designed to facilitate the desired behaviour change. In Zimbabwe, the CGIAR initiative “Transformational Agroecology across Food, Land and Water Systems” in short Agroecology Initiative (AE-I) is working in the Mbire and Murehwa districts (Figure 1). Mbire is in Zimbabwe’s Mashonaland West province and Zimbabwe’s Agroecological Region 4, suitable for livestock and fodder crop production, forestry, and tourism (Chimonyo et al., 2022a). In contrast, Murehwa is in Mashonaland East province, located in Agroecological Region 2, which supports crop and intensive livestock production(Chimonyo et al., 2022b). Agricultural production in both districts has been adversely affected by climate change-induced droughts, and floods in Mbire, increasing food insecurity and eroding community resilience. Research and development organizations have implemented various resilience-building projects based on some agroecological principles in the past few decades (Voss et al., 2023). Though the policy environment supports agroecology, the farmers are attached to their conventional ways of farming, which can slow agroecology adoption (Chiduwa et al., 2022). The AE-I in Zimbabwe is structured around four Agroecological Living Landscapes (ALLs) in Mbire (wards 2 and 3) and Murehwa (wards 4 and 27) and around two value chains, sorghum and poultry, respectively. The ALLs comprise diverse stakeholders whose actions are directly and indirectly linked to food systems. Each ALL has articulated a vision statement covering increased production, increased incomes (through better markets), improved natural resource management, stronger institutions, and household and community peace. Following a diagnostic phase that evaluated the contexts of Mbire and Murehwa and the farmers’ needs, the AE-I team and partners have implemented activities to support increased production around two prioritized value chains. These activities, in part, support the articulated visions and include selected FSA to contextualize the activities.

Methodology To address the four objectives a qualitative research approach was adopted and carried out in five steps: a desk review of project documents, data collection in Mbire and Murehwa, data collection from the AE-I team, behaviour change data analysis, and MEL plan development. Step 1: Initial Desk Review and Analysis of target behaviours and actors A desk review and document analysis were conducted to address Objective 1. Between 2021 and 2024, the Agroecology Initiative (AE-I) Zimbabwe team produced over 40 reports, blogs, and articles. The Vision to Action (V2A) report (Sibanda et al., 2024) outlines the visions of the ALL and serves as a blueprint for agroecology transitions. It identifies activities, actors, and gaps between the stated vision and implemented activities. This study utilizes the V2A report to map activity, actor, and behaviour gaps essential for achieving a sustainable agroecology transition. Guided by the V2A, the focus behaviours were categorized into five groups: (i) achieving high levels of crop and livestock production, (ii) increasing farmers’ access to functional markets, (iii) effective natural resource management, (iv) enhancing active community participation, and (v) addressing gender-based violence (GBV). Actors were grouped into four stakeholder categories: governance, development partners, private sector, and community, with further subdivisions based on similar mandates or value chains. Data was coded as “YES” if a stakeholder was mentioned or participated in activities linked to a report, and “NO” if they were not. The frequency of “YES” was tallied across all work packages, including diagnostic (2, 4, 5) and implementation (1, 3) packages. Underrepresented stakeholders were identified by comparing the average number of mentions or participation instances in AE-I reports and ALL activities. The identified actors were categorised into three groups: (i) those who participated at least five times, (ii) those who participated less than five times, and (iii) those who did not participate. Actors were ranked based on their interests, influence, and potential to drive behaviour change. This classification of behaviours and actors guided the development of questions for key informant interviews and the identification of new actors for Step 2. Step 2: Data collection in Mbire and Murehwa Fieldwork was conducted to validate desk review findings and address Objective 2. In August 2024, the Initiative engaged the Mbire and Murehwa ALLs to confirm the visions outlined in the V2A report. During this period, gaps related to actors, activities, and required behaviour changes were identified. Data collection involved observing ALL meetings and conducting key informant interviews (KIIs) with randomly selected male and female farmers and stakeholders. The data focused on AE-I interventions, ALL visions, farmers’ enablers and barriers to participation, and their expectations of other stakeholders in the V2A. The snowball method was used to identify potential ALL stakeholders mentioned in project documents or by farmers but not previously engaged. Additional data was gathered in September 2024 from randomly selected ALL farmers, non-ALL farmers, and non-farmer actors to characterise gaps identified in the desk review (Table 1). This included assessing the relevance of non-engaged actors to the transition pathways in the V2A, their potential to collaborate with the Initiative, their proposed contributions, and desired changes to the local food system. Step 3: Data collection from members of the AE-I Zimbabwe team Data was collected from selected team members involved in implementing activities across the five work packages. Online interviews were conducted in October 2024 to explore the logic and assumptions underlying the intervention design, addressing Objective 3.

The Initiative has built awareness about agroecology to help farmers transition by providing training and interactive platforms such as field days and fairs. Engaging development partners in the next business cycle could bring additional resources and expertise to address these challenges. A strategic approach to this engagement would ensure alignment with community priorities and AEI principles, fostering synergies that empower farmers to achieve their goals sustainably. Emerging gaps linked to the farmers’ desire to achieve farming excellence A key finding from the KIIs was the slow pace of agroecology transitions, aligning with observations from Work Package 5. KIIs identified several reasons for this gradual progress. Farmers and ALL stakeholders expressed mixed perceptions; supporters emphasised benefits such as higher yields, reduced input requirements, and inclusivity for those without draught power. Critics described agroecology as labour-intensive and referred to it as a "poor person’s alternative," citing issues like limited basin diggers and mulch scarcity. The Initiative could address these challenges by countering negative perceptions through examples of successful wealthy farmers practising agroecology at scale, providing additional training and seasonal modelling to build confidence, and promoting live mulching with incentives such as small seed packs for alternative mulch crops. Collaborating with seed companies like Easi Seeds and SeedCo could further enhance participation in Mbire and Murehwa, extending beyond Agroecology fairs. Key informant interviews at the Initiative level proposed further research to investigate the reasons behind the gradual pace of agroecology transitions. This research would focus on three key areas: first, identifying the specific needs, capabilities, and priorities of women and men across different age groups to tailor activities effectively; second, analysing how behaviour change occurs at various stages of agroecology trajectories, including the impact of farmers’ intersectional identities—such as gender, socioeconomic status, and education levels—on their power and agency to share knowledge within their networks; and third, examining the root causes of barriers to agroecology adoption in smallholder farming systems in Mbire and Murehwa to address challenges more effectively.

Page | 8 4.2 Behavioural Category 2: Increase farmers’ access to functional markets Market access is a critical factor influencing farmers’ income potential, aspirations, and strategies for achieving economic resilience. Farmers in the ALLs aim to enhance market access to boost their incomes, with each ALL encountering distinct opportunities and challenges based on their geographical and infrastructural contexts. Farmers in Mbire face limited market access and often sell their produce to middlemen who supply markets in Harare and occasionally Mozambique. In contrast, the ALLs in Murehwa benefit from better market access due to their proximity to Harare but seek improved market prices and access to international markets. Their vision is to transition from predominantly informal markets to formal and export markets that offer higher prices. The V2A process and KIIs identified activities conducted by the Initiative and its partners, including training on market access, business models, value addition, and contract farming. These efforts align with the ALLs' vision of increasing production for target markets. Partners and stakeholders have supported the development of viable, context-specific business models tailored to the sorghum value chain in Mbire and the poultry value chain in Murehwa. In Mbire, efforts focus on strengthening the sorghum value chain through contract farming. PHI, a key partner, sources markets for farmers, reducing their reliance on middlemen. Farmers' prior experience with contract farming for cotton has eased their transition to similar arrangements for sorghum. These efforts aim to streamline market access and improve farmers' income potential. However, there is a need to enhance farmers’ capacity to negotiate contracts and identify diverse markets to ensure sustainability. In Murehwa, the Initiative has focused on developing the poultry value chain, starting with production. Hamara, a partner organisation, has supported the rearing of SASSO chicks, encouraging farmers to create informal, community- based market systems driven by shared interests. Farmers in Murehwa, already experienced in poultry farming, have adapted to raising SASSO chicks more easily. One notable intervention involved encouraging farmers to establish businesses where chicks are raised to four weeks of age before being sold. While promising, this model has faced challenges in identifying viable markets, leading some farmers to pivot to egg production or selling mature chickens. These farmers now compete with traditional and broiler chicken producers, highlighting the need for additional capacity-building efforts to improve market access. Both value chains underscore the importance of strengthening market linkages, supporting collective farmer businesses, and enhancing marketing knowledge. Collective action in both districts could reduce transaction costs by enabling farmers to engage with buyers, processors, or other value chain actors as unified groups. Additionally, ongoing efforts to foster trust, collaboration, and capacity for sourcing markets could significantly advance the ALLs' vision for sustainable livelihoods. Emerging gaps linked to Improving farmer capacity to access markets Farmers currently lack a comprehensive understanding of market dynamics, limiting their ability to position themselves effectively. Despite receiving training through the ALLs, many remain unaware of essential market functions, such as price determination, grading criteria, and available selling platforms. This knowledge gap hinders their ability to plan and prepare for market engagement, reducing their competitiveness. Addressing this through targeted capacity-building initiatives can equip farmers with the tools needed to navigate markets confidently and secure fair prices. A lack of strategic production planning and post-harvest management further challenges farmers' ability to align activities with market demands. KIIs revealed that farmers often fail to synchronise production cycles with periods of low supply, limiting their capacity to command better prices. Income stress during harvest periods and inadequate infrastructure for drying and storage exacerbate these issues. Collaboration with

Collaboration with private sector partners is essential to provide the necessary assets, skills, and knowledge. For example, stakeholders like SNV1, which builds smallholders’ capacity to engage in markets in Zimbabwe, can play a significant role. Establishing collective farmer organisations or cooperatives could reduce transaction costs and improve farmers’ bargaining power. Building long-term, sustainable relationships between farmers and market actors is crucial for securing reliable income sources. Frameworks that facilitate continuous engagement with buyers, processors, and other value chain participants are essential. Encouraging adherence to contract requirements and emphasizing mutual benefits can foster trust and collaboration. Partnerships that assist farmers in analysing markets and positioning themselves for emerging opportunities will further support growth and adaptability. Training builds on traditional knowledge about edible and medicinal products held by older community members near forests. There is potential to expand by collaborating with development organisations and small businesses to process and market non-timber forest products, such as honey. Branding these products as sustainably sourced from Mbire and Murehwa could diversify incomes and improve livelihoods. As the Initiative progresses through the V2A process, sustained collaboration with communities is vital. Establishing an environment that supports sustainable practices and promotes fair institutions will ensure long-term outcomes. Engaging both current and new stakeholders to deliver these messages to diverse age groups is crucial. Key informant interviews revealed that youth and school children occasionally start fires, highlighting the importance of targeted awareness campaigns. Customised messaging for different age groups can drive community- wide behaviour change, reducing incidents of fires and tree cutting. Co-existence with wildlife in Mbire Human-wildlife conflict (HWC) remains a significant challenge for the ALLs in Mbire, particularly in Wards 2 and 3, which border game parks. These proximity dynamics result in frequent interactions with wildlife such as elephants, lions, and buffalos. While these animals are integral to the ecosystem and the region's biodiversity, they threaten farmers' livelihoods by endangering their safety, damaging crops, and preying on livestock. he V2A framework highlights community safety, enforcement mechanisms, and financial resources as critical for coexistence. However, these issues often cause tension between communities and authorities. Mbire RDC and ZimParks train communities to deter elephants and build enclosures for livestock protection, yet these measures prove inadequate as wildlife adapts, placing farmers at risk of injury or death while guarding their crops. Anti-poaching laws enforced by ZimParks and the council sometimes penalise communities defending themselves against attacks. Disproportionate responses to incidents, with stronger reactions to the killing of animals compared to harm to humans, crops, or livestock, exacerbate tensions. Both communities and authorities lack adequate resources. Communities often use CAMPFIRE funds for other projects, leaving scouts without necessary equipment like rifles and bullets. Similarly, ZimParks and council officials face resource constraints, including insufficient funding for ranger equipment and vehicle maintenance, leading to delayed responses. A draft bill addressing compensation for human-wildlife conflict victims is under consideration. Finalising this bill could encourage behavioural change by providing communities with a safety net.

Emerging Gaps Linked to Effective Natural Resource Management Effective natural resource management in the ALLs faces critical challenges, and The Initiative's current interventions leave several gaps that need addressing. One major issue is streambank cultivation, which continues as a necessity for communities lacking alternative irrigation water sources. While The Initiative has raised awareness about environmental risks, such as river siltation, it has not yet introduced sustainable alternatives to meet irrigation needs. Without tangible solutions like improved irrigation infrastructure or watershed restoration, communities will unlikely transition away from this practice, perpetuating environmental degradation. The absence of a compensation mechanism for wildlife-related losses highlights a significant gap in the Initiative's approach. Wildlife attacks repeatedly erode farmers’ asset bases, causing food insecurity and increasing vulnerability to future shocks. While the Initiative has engaged stakeholders in raising awareness about HWC, it has not actively facilitated the development of compensation schemes or mechanisms to mitigate the economic impacts of these conflicts. This oversight leaves communities without recourse for losses, undermining their trust in conservation efforts and increasing resistance to wildlife preservation. Human settlements within wildlife corridors remain a persistent challenge, but The Initiative has yet to address this issue effectively at a landscape level. Although stakeholders have identified the risks of HWC for communities residing in these zones, efforts to prevent further settlement have been limited. The Initiative has not adequately engaged in participatory landscape planning or provided incentives and support systems to encourage improved land planning. This gap leaves communities in high-risk areas with few options, perpetuating cycles of conflict and environmental strain. The Initiative’s interventions also lack integration of landscape management principles. Current efforts focus on individual practices, such as raising awareness and training, but do not address the broader need for coordinated landscape-scale solutions. For instance, strategies like agroforestry, land-use zoning, and habitat restoration have not been fully leveraged to create synergies between conservation and livelihoods. These gaps highlight the need for more comprehensive, multi-stakeholder approaches considering ecological, social, and economic dynamics across the ALLs. To address these gaps, the Initiative must prioritize practical, scalable solutions that align with community needs. This includes alternative irrigation systems, wildlife compensation mechanisms, and participatory landscape planning to manage human settlements and wildlife corridors effectively. Closing these gaps will strengthen resilience, equity and sustainable resource management across the ALLs. 4.4 Behavioural Category 4: Active participation among community members The ALLs in Mbire’s vision extend to increasing active participation in decision-making and increasing the number of women in leadership. A consequence of this very traditional environment is few women are in leadership and rarely participate actively in community decision-making platforms. However, key informant interviews from the other three ALLs confirm that GBV is a widespread issue. Community members in all four ALLs identified financial stress, food insecurity, and the misuse of farming income as triggers for GBV. Despite these triggers, GBV stems primarily from gender inequality and social norms that tolerate violence against women (Castañeda Carney et al., 2020). Partners such as the Ministry of Women Affairs and traditional leaders are leading efforts to address this issue, but transforming social norms remains a gradual process. The V2A lists knowledge and social norms as essential to stopping GBV. The KIIs suggest additional measures, including community workshops, economic empowerment, and reporting offenders to traditional authorities and ZRP. The Ministry of Women’s Affairs has trained the ALLs on gender mainstreaming in agriculture and income generation, demonstrating that increasing women’s access to and control over resources benefits households. At the community level, representatives from the Ministry of Women’s Affairs, traditional leaders, and gender-focused NGOs adopt a multi-faceted approach to addressing GBV. They emphasise livelihood diversification, non-violent conflict management, and respect for women’s rights. These principles are reflected in the ALLs, where an environment of mutual respect and non- violent conflict resolution is fostered.

Page | 29 9. FURTHER RESEARCH Members of the Initiative reflected on future research ideas that they could pursue going forward. The research ideas fall in four main themes: increasing agroecology transitions, increasing market access, GBV and policy. Future research on agroecology transitions should focus on: • Identifying the positive tipping points and entry points to agroecology and levers for adoption which we can use to scale up. The focus could move from the farm level, to the landscape allowing for measurement of progress at the individual farmer and community levels. • The food system experiences uncertainty from different factors, such as climate change, economic downturns and war, the V2A, should consider agroecological transitions under different scenarios, so farmers can adopt more robust strategies. • Water availability and water use efficiency for agroecology – which infield water harvesting strategies can maximize the volumes of water we harvest and how can we track its use • What role can mechanical implements play in the agroecology transition? Are there gender/socioeconomic opportunities and constraints to access and use certain implements? Future research on Policy should focus on: • Which of the 13 agroecology principles should policymakers first target to promote agroecology interventions? • How can local governance actors support agroecology transitions? • To determine the changes in the broader policy context, which will increase people’s capacity to choose agroecology, for example, payments for ecosystem services to make agroecology a viable choice. Future research on Market Access should focus on: • What business and finance models would make the most sense for farmers and businesses? • What role can non-traditional finance models, like village savings and lending societies have on agroecology transitions in Mbire and Murehwa? • How can we pay for ecosystem services to incentivize farmers and businesses to engage in agroecology? Future research on Stop GBV should focus on: • What are the drivers of GBV, and how do we intervene without making these worse? How do we empower women without contributing to violence? • Why are GBV enforcement mechanisms inadequate, and what pathways can the AE- I adopt to increase community benefits and buy-in? 10. CONCLUSION The Initiative’s implementation activities, along with those of ALL stakeholders, are largely aligned with the V2A plans for the four ALLs. Significant progress has been made in supporting

Page | 30 farmers to increase production, which is a crucial step toward improving market access. Partners have also begun engaging in natural resource management, enhancing community participation, and addressing gender inequality and GBV. While behaviour change requires time, the groundwork laid so far provides a strong foundation for the critical efforts needed in the next business cycle. A stakeholder mapping exercise identified 472 actors across the governance, private sector, development, and community sectors. Thus far, efforts have primarily focused on engaging the governance and community sectors. Limited engagement with private sector actors has played a critical role in mechanisation and developing sorghum and poultry value chains. Opportunities have been identified to further collaborate with the development and private sectors, particularly to enhance market readiness, improve market sourcing, promote active participation in decision-making, and address gender inequality and GBV.
