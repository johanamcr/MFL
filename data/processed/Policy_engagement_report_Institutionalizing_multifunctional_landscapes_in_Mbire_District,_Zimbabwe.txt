Executive Summary This report presents the outcomes of a district-level policy engagement held in Mbire District, Zimbabwe, under the CGIAR Multifunctional Landscapes (MFL) Program within Area of Work 4: Institutions and Policies. The engagement sought to identify practical mechanisms for embedding MFL principles into local governance systems, particularly District Development Plans (DDPs), by-laws, and coordination platforms. The meeting brought together the Mbire Rural District Council (RDC), the District Development Coordinator’s Office, sectoral departments (EMA, Forestry Commission, Parks and Wildlife, ARDAS, ZINWA), traditional leaders, community representatives, and development partners including CIMMYT, DanChurchAid, and Fambidzanayi. The engagement established a shared understanding of the MFL approach-an integrated framework promoting balance between agricultural production, conservation, and human well-being-and analyzed the alignment of district policies and by-laws with national frameworks such as the Environmental Management Act (Chapter 20:27), Forest Act, Parks and Wildlife Management Act, National Wetlands Policy (2022), and the National Agricultural Policy Framework (2018-2030). Participants acknowledged that while these policies exist, implementation remains fragmented due to overlapping mandates, limited enforcement capacity, and inadequate coordination. Two thematic groups deliberated on (i) Natural Resources Management and By-laws, and (ii) Cross-Cutting Enablers covering policy alignment, gender and inclusion, capacity, and financing. Key outcomes included calls to harmonize and update local by-laws; strengthen the Mbire Natural Resources Management Platform for inter- sectoral coordination; expand investment in water infrastructure and irrigation; integrate modern technologies such as drones for environmental monitoring; and establish incentive systems rewarding conservation and sustainable practices. The discussions also emphasized inclusive participation of women, youth, and marginalized groups, as well as the introduction of livestock insurance and local processing industries to link conservation with livelihoods. The engagement demonstrated how district-level participatory governance can operationalize the MFL Program’s objectives, contributing directly to Output 4.1 (co-developed policy options and enacting strategies) and Output 4.3 (governance and coordination mechanisms). It reinforced that institutionalizing MFL is not a legislative event, but an adaptive process anchored in local planning cycles, inclusive decision making, and practical livelihood incentives that make sustainability attainable for communities.

Introduction and Context The CGIAR Multifunctional Landscapes (MFL) Program seeks to transform land, water, and food systems by promoting integrated management of landscapes that deliver social, economic, and ecological benefits (CGIAR,2024). Under AoW 4, the program supports national and subnational institutions to design and enact policies, governance structures, and coordination mechanisms that foster multifunctionality. Setting the context of the program within the Mbire MFL Mbire District, located in northern Zimbabwe, represents a critical landscape for this agenda. The district’s semi- arid conditions, fragile ecosystems, and high reliance on natural resources make it a key site for advancing integrated governance models (Chimonyo et al, 2023). However, overlapping institutional mandates, weak enforcement of environmental by-laws, and limited community inclusion have constrained sustainable resource management. Policies and by-laws regulating natural resources are in place but are fragmented, operate in silos and often in collision. The Environmental Management Act (Chapter 20:27), the Forest Act, Parks and Wildlife Management Act, and local environmental by-laws exist but overlap in scope and enforcement, constraining progress on the ground. The policy engagement reported herein sought to identify how they can be better aligned to amplify synergies and reduce conflicts with a bias towards multifunctional landscapes. As a secondary outcome, the engagements aimed to create a platform to mainstream the MFL approach into actionable local governance reforms by identifying practical entry points through for example, district development plans (DDPs), by-laws, and coordination platforms such as the Mbire Natural Resources Management Committee. Specifically, engagements sought to: • Create a shared understanding of the MFL approach and its relevance to district development priorities. • Review existing policies, by-laws, and institutional arrangements affecting natural resource management. • Identify entry points for integrating MFL into DDPs, by-laws, and coordination mechanisms. • Explore enablers such as capacity, financing, and inclusion for sustainable implementation. Engagement Approach and Methodology The engagement was conducted as a participatory workshop facilitated by CIMMYT and IWMI with support from Mbire RDC. It brought together government departments, local authorities, development partners, and community representatives. Information. The engagements were organized around three sessions. Session 1 included introductions and context setting, session 2 was for group discussions on natural resources management and cross-cutting enablers such as policy, GEYSI, capacity, financing and session 3 was for plenary presentations and synthesis of key insights from the group discussions. This co-creation approach fostered inclusive dialogue and grounded the discussion in lived realities from across sectors and communities. Engagement Proceedings and Discussion Highlights Setting the Context and MFL Concept Opening remarks from district authorities emphasized the need for coherent resource management. The District Development Coordinator noted, "The landscape is our economy; if we manage it well, we can address both food security and environmental sustainability." The facilitation team introduced the concept of multifunctional landscapes, explaining its focus on balancing production and conservation goals and integrating multiple ecosystem services for multiple users (Reed et al., 2020; Sayer et al., 2013). “A multifunctional landscape is one where farming, biodiversity, and livelihoods coexist productively rather than compete,” the facilitator explained. The approach emphasizes inclusivity, local agency, and co-learning among smallholders, extension officers, researchers, and policy actors. • Livestock Management and Control By-law • Agricultural By-law • Vegetation and Fire Prevention By-law • Public Health, Meat, and Anti-Litter By-laws Participants noted that many of these by-laws are outdated and poorly enforced. "We can’t enforce by-laws if people don’t have water," remarked one ARDAS officer, referring to the challenge of restricting streambank cultivation when upland irrigation systems are absent. The RDC acknowledged overlaps among departments and outdated provisions that do not reflect current agroecological challenges. The RDC presentation further highlighted that socio- economic activities-livestock rearing, crop production, beekeeping, fishing, and safari hunting depend heavily on natural resource sustainability, reinforcing the need for integrated management frameworks Group 1: Natural Resources Management and By-laws Farmer presenting findings from their groupwork Group 1 had a discussion on identifying gaps, overlaps, and opportunities for strengthening local by-laws that govern natural resource management in Mbire District. The discussion revealed that while numerous by-laws exist, their implementation remains weak and fragmented, largely due to institutional, social, and economic constraints. Participants highlighted several gaps and overlaps in the current regulatory framework. A recurring concern was the absence of compensation mechanisms for farmers whose crops are destroyed by wildlife, which has led to growing frustration and disincentives for conservation. One farmer expressed this sentiment clearly, noting that “we are told to protect wildlife, but when elephants destroy our crops, there is no one to help us recover.” Recognizing this long- standing gap, DanChurchAid shared that they have begun piloting a Human Wildlife Conflict insurance product in 2025. Although still in its infancy, the initiative has already brought key stakeholders together to design a viable compensation model, and participants noted that once fully developed such a product could significantly cushion farmers from wildlife-related losses and strengthen community willingness to support conservation efforts. The group also observed that community consultation during policy and by-law formulation is limited, often restricted to councilors and village heads, with minimal ward-level participation. Farmers expressed frustration over limited consultation. “Policies are made for us but without us,” said one farmer representative, as the policy makers only bring representatives like one per ward, or consult councilors. Farmers were arguing that councilors often represent communities in policy meetings without giving proper feedback afterward. Additionally, there is councilors’ reluctance to enforce strict environmental regulations due to political considerations, since they were voted into office Participants further noted that weak enforcement of existing by-laws stems from limited institutional capacity and resource constraints. Environmental officers lack adequate transport, monitoring tools, and logistical support to implement the laws effectively. Fragmented institutional mandates across departments were highlighted as leading

to duplication and inaction, for instance one might get a permit to cut trees, but there is no follow up to check if they are respecting other bylaws and policies in the process. To address these challenges, the group proposed several by-law strengthening measures. These included tree planting (fruit and adapted trees fruit trees like ‘Ziziphus Mauritiana’) along riverbanks and riparian zones to prevent soil erosion, and the development of piped-water schemes and boreholes to provide alternative irrigation options, thereby reducing the need for streambank cultivation. Participants emphasized the importance of awareness campaigns on sustainable resource management and called for benefit-sharing mechanisms to ensure that communities participating in conservation efforts receive tangible rewards. On community engagement, the group underscored the need for active inclusion of youth, women, and traditional leaders in environmental committees, with balanced representation across gender and age groups. While communities expressed strong support for conservation, they also emphasized the need for practical livelihood alternatives. “People understand why we must protect the environment,” noted one ARDAS Officer, “but they also need ways to survive while doing it.” This aptly captures the classic conservation dilemma where conservation is pitted against food production. Initiatives such as Village Business Units (VBUs), fish farming, and small livestock integration were identified as viable strategies for enhancing resilience and reducing dependence on environmentally harmful practices. Regarding capacity gaps, the group pointed to inadequate access to water infrastructure, limited community level awareness, and insufficient tools for monitoring compliance. Participants called for the establishment of livestock auction centers and loan facilities to improve income opportunities and reduce overexploitation of natural resources. They also raised concern over the continuous extraction of groundwater without replenishment, warning that it posed a long-term risk to ecosystem sustainability. As one Fambidzanayi representative remarked, “we are drawing water faster than nature can replace it soon, even boreholes will run dry.” Group 2: Cross-Cutting Enablers (Policy, GEYSI, Capacity, Financing) Farmer presenting findings from their groupwork The second group, which focused on cross-cutting enablers such as policy, capacity, gender equality youth and social inclusion (GEYSI), and financing, examined the broader systemic and institutional factors influencing the integration of the MFL approach into district development processes. Participants observed that limited resources for veldfire monitoring and enforcement, coupled with conflicting sectoral priorities between environmental protection and agricultural production, often hindered effective coordination among departments. They also noted weak feedback mechanisms from national and provincial offices to local communities, as well as low policy awareness among citizens, which undermines local ownership of environmental initiatives. Despite these challenges, the group recognized areas of alignment with existing DDP priorities. Environmental subcommittees already monitor land-use practices, while ARDAS and development partners continue to support VBUs and livelihood diversification. Community scouts are also actively engaged in educating residents on sustainable livestock and environmental management, providing a strong foundation for institutionalizing MFL principles at the district level.

Looking ahead, the group proposed several considerations for the next DDP cycle. These included increased investment in water sources and smallholder micro irrigation schemes at district level to address water scarcity, and the promotion of learning exchanges and “look-and-learn” visits to enhance experiential learning among farmers and local leaders. Participants called for the introduction of high-value crops, coupled with incentive systems to reward wards demonstrating exemplary conservation and sustainable farming practices. They emphasized the importance of continuous follow-up on the adoption of new technologies, bottom-up consultations where policymakers engage communities directly, and regular feedback meetings and monitoring frameworks to strengthen accountability. Furthermore, they proposed the use of drones and other modern technologies for environmental monitoring and anti-poaching, as well as the establishment of local processing industries, such as a non-timber forest product factory at Mushumbi, to promote value addition and local employment. On gender and inclusion, participants stressed that "women, youth, and marginalized groups must be in leadership, not just in attendance." They noted that barriers such as low educational attainment and harsh working conditions often discourage women from taking leadership roles and called for deliberate measures to address these constraints. On financing options, the group proposed the promotion of livestock insurance products to protect farmers from losses, the establishment of local abattoirs and livestock marketing centres to improve market access, and stronger enforcement of theft and resource use laws to safeguard community assets and encourage responsible resource management. Thematic Analysis of Emerging Issues and Opportunities Institutional Gaps and Overlaps Discussions revealed fragmented responsibilities among key institutions EMA, Forestry Commission, Parks and Wildlife, and ARDAS, with each operating under different legal frameworks. The absence of a coordination mechanism results in inconsistent enforcement and duplication. One council official observed, “Sometimes we are all doing the same awareness campaigns but never meet to compare notes.” The Mbire Natural Resources Management Committee was identified as a potential coordinating vehicle. Capacity and Resource Constraints Across departments, significant capacity gaps including lack of transport, understaffed offices, and inadequate monitoring equipment were repeatedly highlighted as undermining effective enforcement of by-laws. At community level, the absence of adequate information, extension support, and environmental training further weakens sustainable resource management. As one villager explained, “We know we should protect trees, but we also need wood to cook,” underscoring the practical trade-offs households must navigate. Social Inclusion and Local Agency The engagement revealed uneven participation across gender and age groups. Traditional leaders often dominate committees, while youth and women remain underrepresented. Participants proposed mandating inclusive representation and leadership rotation to improve diversity. Governance and Coordination Mechanisms There was consensus that local platforms must be empowered to lead cross-sectoral planning and monitoring. The Mbire Natural Resources Management Committee could be strengthened into a district-level landscape coordination node, aligning with AoW 4, Output 4.3 on governance and coordination mechanisms. The “So What?” Discussion The Mbire engagement provided clear evidence of how district-level participatory governance can enable institutionalization of the MFL vision. It illustrated that institutionalizing the MFL approach requires strengthening governance systems rather than drafting new legislation. It highlighted the following: 1. Local governance drives institutionalization: Local authorities and traditional leaders play a pivotal role in translating MFL principles into enforceable by-laws and participatory land use decisions. RDC’s willingness to integrate MFL into the next DDP cycle demonstrates how decentralized authorities can translate landscape- level research into administrative policy. This reflects the MFL Program’s emphasis on bottom-up policy processes where subnational actors co-develop and enact sustainable resource management strategies.

2. Institutional coordination enables landscape governance: The engagement demonstrated the potential of district-level platforms, such as the Mbire Natural Resources Management Committee as a tangible avenue for operationalizing AoW 4.3, by transforming fragmented mandates into collaborative landscape management. These forums foster cross-sector dialogue and joint accountability which are key attributes of AoW 4’s vision of polycentric governance. 3. Inclusion and agency enable institutional transformation: The engagement validated the MFL assumption that sustainable landscapes depend on inclusive governance (IPBES, 2019). The deliberate inclusion of women, youth, and marginalized groups responds directly to AoW 4’s requirement for fairness and participation in governance systems. It also enhances accountability and equity, aligning with the GEYSI. Strengthening agency at the local level ensures long-term institutional sustainability of MFL interventions. 4. Livelihood incentives bridge policy and practice: The introduction of livelihood enabling measures such as livestock insurance, small processing industries, and local markets reflects the recognition that environmental stewardship must coexist with economic viability. DanChurchAid further underscored the need for multi-peril crop insurance that includes coverage for wildlife-induced crop damage, noting that such mechanisms would significantly reduce farmer vulnerability and strengthen incentives for compliance with natural resource by- laws. These actions link policy to practice, transforming conservation principles into community-relevant incentives. Collectively, these insights illustrate that institutionalizing MFL is not a legislative event, but an adaptive governance process that can be anchored in district planning cycles, social inclusion, and iterative learning. It demonstrates that institutionalizing MFL is a process of strengthening governance systems not merely adopting new policies. This engagement bridged science, policy, and community perspectives, laying a foundation for sustained institutional transformation. Lessons and Reflections • Coordination across sectors is vital for multifunctional landscape outcomes. • Policy instruments are effective only when matched with practical livelihood support. Thus, linking livelihood options to conservation goals enhances compliance and sustainability • Social inclusion enhances compliance and innovation in resource management. Gender-sensitive representation fosters balanced decision-making and intergenerational stewardship. • Regular feedback between community and government builds trust and accountability. Continuous learning and evidence feedback are required to sustain institutional momentum. • Incremental integration of MFL into DDPs builds ownership and legitimacy among district actors and ensures sustainability of institutional reforms. Conclusion and Recommendations Conclusion The policy engagement in Mbire District marked an important milestone in advancing the institutionalization of Multifunctional Landscapes in Zimbabwe. It established a shared understanding, identified practical governance entry points and pathways for embedding it in local governance systems. It reaffirmed the role of local institutions in steering sustainable resource management and underscored that sustainable landscape management depends on inclusive, coordinated, and well-resourced institutions. As part of CGIAR’s Multifunctional Landscapes Program (AoW 4: Institutions and Policies), this engagement offers an important case of how decentralized policy processes can deliver tangible institutional transformation. The process highlights that building multifunctional landscapes is as much about institutional relationships as it is about ecological restoration. Building on this foundation, future work will focus on supporting implementation, documenting learning, and scaling these governance models across other Zimbabwean landscapes. Recommendations 1. Integrate MFL principles into the next DDP review cycle.
