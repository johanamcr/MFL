1 Executive Summary As part of CIMMYT’s mandate to strengthen technical capacity within the Water & Soil Accelerator (WSA) Program, a Training of Trainers (ToT) was conducted from 23–27 October 2025 targeting technical, research, and extension staff from the two consortia they are supporting, namely Conservation Farming Unit (CFU), and Total LandCare (TLC), with their collaborating partners along, including Farmers’ Outgrower Foundation (FoF), African Fertilizer and Agribusiness Partnership (AFAP), Pelum, and the Ministry of Agriculture. The overall goal was to standardize the implementation of the 2025/26 WSA Mega Demonstration Protocols and to equip partners with hands-on skills in Conservation Agriculture (CA), basin digger technology, and legume seed multiplication and management. The trainings also aimed to strengthen data quality, consistency, and inclusivity across implementation areas in Zambia and Malawi. Three ToT sessions were held across the two countries: • Session 1: CFU staff training in Chongwe District, Central Zambia (16 participants – 38 % women, 44 % youth). • Session 2: TLC (Zambia) and FoF staff training at Msekera Research Station, Chipata, Eastern Zambia (7 participants – 29 % women, 43 % youth). • Session 3: TLC (Malawi), AFAP, Pelum, and Ministry of Agriculture staff training in Mchinji District, Malawi (22 participants – 27 % women, 22 % youth). The training combined classroom presentations, practical field demonstrations, and interactive group discussions. It focused on the design, layout, and management of demonstration plots; legume seed multiplication, and strengthening data management, in accordance with the training schedule outlined in the WSA work plan. Key outcomes include: • 45 technical staff trained on standardized CA demonstration establishment (EG.3.2- 24). • Improved partner capacity to apply WSA data protocols and digital monitoring tools (HL.8.5-2). • Strengthened legume seed systems and quality-declared seed (QDS) knowledge (CUST-1). • Enhanced women and youth participation, with gender and age disaggregation integrated into MELIA tracking (CUST-2). • Greater awareness of basin digger business opportunities for local mechanization service providers (EG.3.2-24 and EG.3.2-25). • Deeper understanding of the need for robust data management

Background The Water & Soil Accelerator (WSA) Program, implemented by IWMI in partnership with CIMMYT, TLC, CFU, and FoF, aims to accelerate the adoption of climate-smart soil and water management (SWM) practices among smallholder farmers in Zambia and Malawi. The program contributes to the CGIAR Sustainable Farming Program by enhancing the resilience of farming systems through innovations that improve water infiltration, soil health, crop productivity, and selection of drought-tolerant varieties. CIMMYT provided scientific leadership within the consortium by developing field implementation protocols, coordinating agronomic research, building partner capacity to apply evidence-based approaches in the field, and generating additional robust empirical evidence on this activity. One of the core outputs under Outcome 2 (adoption of CSA/SWM practices) is training trainers (ToT) for implementation partners to ensure that demonstration plots and data collection processes follow standardized, research-backed methodologies. Therefore, the 2025 ToT sessions form part of CIMMYT’s planned technical backstopping activities to prepare partner organizations for the 2025/26 demonstration season. They are anchored in the consortium’s joint work plan and directly contribute to MELIA indicators EG.3.2 (individuals trained), EG.3.2-24 (farmers applying improved technologies), and EG.3.2- 25 (hectares under improved management). The trainings align with the consortium’s ongoing commitment to improving data quality, gender and youth inclusion, and evidence-based learning, all of which are priorities highlighted in recent WSA program assessments. 2. Objectives of the Training The ToT was designed to: 1. Strengthen partners’ technical capacity to establish and manage standardized WSA Mega Demonstrations. 2. Build partner confidence in using basin digger technology for smallholder mechanization. 3. Enhance knowledge on legume seed production, field management, and quality assurance and quality control (QC/QA) of legume seed production.

Methodology and Training Approach The Training of Trainers (ToT) adopted a co-learning and participatory approach that blended theory, demonstration, and practice. The methodology emphasized experiential learning, partner interaction, and hands-on engagement to ensure that participants could apply the skills in their own field contexts immediately after training. Each training session combined classroom instruction, group discussions, and field demonstrations to reinforce understanding of the technical and operational components of the Water & Soil Accelerator (WSA) Mega Demonstration Protocol. CIMMYT facilitators led participants through four integrated modules, supported by partner specialists from TLC, AFAP, and the Ministry of Agriculture: 1. CA Principles and Demonstration Protocols – This section introduced the core pillars of Conservation Agriculture, namely minimum soil disturbance, crop residue retention, and crop diversification. Participants analyzed the WSA Mega Demo layout (nine treatments) and discussed different agronomic aspects such as fertilizer management, residue allocation, and treatment replication across sites. 2. Basin Digger Operation and Maintenance – a practical module covered assembly, calibration, servicing, and use of the mechanical basin digger. The session also included safety checks, ergonomic handling, and field demonstrations on how to make uniform basins. Participants discussed challenges such as drill-bit length/ height, ideal basin spacing, and potential for local service provision. 3. Legume Seed Multiplication and Quality Assurance – focused on field management for the production of quality-declared seed (QDS) production. Quality Declared Seed (QDS) refers to seed produced and distributed under a system that ensures it meets basic standards of quality yet is less demanding than full seed certification schemes enforced by government agencies. Topics included site selection, isolation distances, weed and pest management, gypsum application for calcium nutrition, harvesting, and post-harvest handling. The module aligned with regional seed certification standards. 4. Agronomic Data Collection and Digital Tools – This module emphasized the principles of precision, consistency, and accountability in data management. Participants were trained on identifying key data parameters (yield, biomass, plant population, GPS coordinates, soil cover), quality assurance, and practised using mobile-based data-collection platforms (ODK/KoBo).

4 3.1 Training Tools and Methods A variety of learning tools and facilitation methods were employed to ensure inclusivity, interaction, and practical mastery: • PowerPoint presentations and schematic layouts of mega demonstrations. • Group discussions and Q&A sessions to encourage peer learning and experience sharing. • Hands-on field practice on demonstration setup, basin digger operation, and seed inspection. • Live data-collection simulations using tablets and mobile devices equipped with ODK/KoBo forms. • Reflective sessions after each module to consolidate learning and address partner- specific challenges. All exercises were geo-tagged and disaggregated by sex and age to comply with MELIA indicators EG.3.2, EG.3.2-24, EG.3.2-25, and HL.8.5-2. The participatory nature of the training ensured that partners not only learned standardized procedures but also contributed context-specific insights to adapt the WSA protocols to diverse agroecological and social settings. 4. Training Implementation and Highlights 4.1. Day 1 – 23 October 2025 (CFU Training, Chongwe District) Participants: 16 (6 female, 10 male; 44 % youth). Lead Facilitators: Dr Blessing Mhlanga, Dr Peter Setimela, Dr Mazvita Chiduwa The session led by Dr Blessing Mhlanga opened with a review of the WSA Mega Demonstration Protocol, detailing nine treatment configurations covering: 1. Maize varietal comparisons; 2. Maize–groundnut and maize–cowpea intercrops; 3. Maize–legume rotations; 4. Double- and four-row strip cropping systems. Participants engaged deeply in treatment design, particularly strip cropping, which was new to most field staff. They analyzed spatial arrangements, fertilizer management, and data to be collected, such as yield, biomass, residue cover, and labour use. Dr Peter Setimela led the module on Legume Seed Multiplication, focusing on: • The dominance of informal legume seed systems in SSA (90 %). • Field selection, isolation distance, and roguing.

6 Dr Mazvita Chiduwa played a key role in facilitating dialogue throughout the sessions. She encouraged participants to reflect on implementation challenges, asked probing questions to deepen understanding, and ensured that diverse perspectives, especially from youth and women, were heard. Practical session: Participants assembled and tested the basin digger, marking basins at standard spacing. They raised practical feedback: • The drill bits were too long for shorter users. • There is a preference for wider bits (compared to the 10 cm radius bits used for demonstration) to enable larger basins (to plant dual crops). CIMMYT recommended testing two drill-bit models and adjusting intra-row spacing accordingly. 8 • Best practices: standardization, consistent units, digital forms with drop-down validation, and peer review. Dr Mazvita Chiduwa also presented the module on Legume Seed Multiplication, adapted from Dr Setimela’s presentation, which was done with CFU. The main discussions were centred on: • The challenges associated with legume seed systems, particularly the low multiplication ratio. • Quality assurance during the process of seed multiplication. • A recurring request was for the clarification of the status of “lack of dormancy” stated for groundnut, and how this differs from other crops, which will also germinate under favourable conditions. Practical Field Demonstration: Participants implemented the demo layout in one of the fields at Msekera under CIMMYT guidance. Key lessons discussed: • Orientation of demos relative to roads to attract attention (strip cropping preferred roadside). • Avoiding sites with anthills, trees, or excessive slope. • Engaging lead farmers in plot establishment for ownership. Key Outcomes and Lessons Learned 6.1 Key Outcomes • 45 technical staff trained across Zambia and Malawi (31 % female, 36 % youth) – EG.3.2. • Standardized application of WSA Mega Demo Protocol across partners – EG.3.2-25. • Enhanced capacity on legume seed multiplication (QDS standards) – CUST-1. • Strengthened data management skills through – HL.8.5-2. • Identified business opportunities for mechanization service providers using basin diggers. 6.2 Lessons Learned • Practical learning significantly improves skill retention and confidence. • Localized adaptation (e.g., pigeon pea substitution) enhances technology relevance. • Visibility matters: Demo placement near roads and closer to the farmers drives farmer interest.
